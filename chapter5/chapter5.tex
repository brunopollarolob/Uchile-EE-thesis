\chapter{Testing, validation, and performance results}
\label{chap:results}
This chapter presents the testing, validation, and performance results of the F-engine developed for the CHARTS project. Firstly, the RFI environment at the Laguna Carén site is characterized, informing the design of improved analog filtering to prevent saturation and data corruption. Next, the performance of the LO tone generation module is evaluated, demonstrating its suitability for driving the FDM mixers. Correlation tests using both a common noise source and prototype CHARTS antennas are presented to verify phase coherence and overall system integrity. A solar transit observation is then conducted to demonstrate the end-to-end functionality of the system under real observational conditions. 

It should be noted that most of the tests presented in this chapter were performed with the 8-antenna configuration, as the hardware required for testing with multiple FDM boards (beyond 8 antennas) was not yet available at the time of writing. Additionally, during this period, the system was undergoing a migration from the differential SMA connector architecture to the RJ45-based interface. Nevertheless, the firmware design for the 32-antenna system has been fully implemented (it uses the same architecture tested with the 8-antenna configuration), with only minor adjustments remaining in the first stage of the corner turn and verification of the 100GbE fiber transmission integrity at full data rate.



\section{Site RFI characterization}
\label{sec:rfi_measurements}
On August 28, 2025, a specialized RFI measurement campaign was conducted to evaluate the complete digitization bandwidth (\SI{0}{\mega\hertz}–\SI{2457.6}{\mega\hertz}), aiming to detect potential spectral leaks at higher frequencies and see the impact of local RFI sources in the 300--500~MHz science band. For this analysis, the CHORD feed (an ultra-wideband antenna with $S_{11} < -10$~dB in the 0–\SI{1500}{\mega\hertz} range, see \citealt{MacKay_2023} for detailed feed specifications) was employed alongside the RFSoC~4x2 backend and an amplification box with a well-defined system temperature, ensuring accurate characterization across the entire frequency range. The measurement setup is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:rfi_measurement_setup}. The procedure involved pointing the CHORD feed towards zenith and recording the power spectrum over a 15-minute integration period, with 25~ms of time resolution (dynamic spectra are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dynamic_spectra_15_min}). The recorded data were then calibrated to derive the sky temperature as a function of frequency, accounting for the known system temperature of the amplification box.

The raw power spectra were processed using a radiometric calibration procedure based on the classical \emph{Y-factor} method (see Section 3.6.6 of \citealt{essential_radio_astronomy}). Each dataset consisted of accumulated power spectra (sum of squared magnitudes) from the FFT outputs. The raw accumulated counts for frequency channel $k$, denoted as $D[k]$, were converted to power units in dBm\,MHz$^{-1}$ via:
\begin{equation}
	P[k] = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{D[k]}{N_\mathrm{acc}}\right) - P_\mathrm{cal},
\end{equation}
where $N_\mathrm{acc}$ is the accumulation length (number of spectra averaged in the FPGA), set to 7500 corresponding to a \SI{25}{\milli\second} integration time, and $P_\mathrm{cal}$ is a calibration offset empirically determined by injecting a \SI{400}{\mega\hertz} tone of known power.

To characterize the receiver noise temperature, measurements were performed using a calibrated noise source. We define two states: \emph{hot} (noise source ON) and \emph{cold} (noise source OFF). The effective brightness temperature for the hot state is given by:
\begin{equation}
	T_\mathrm{ON} = T_\mathrm{OFF}\left(10^{\frac{\mathrm{ENR}}{10}} + 1\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{ENR}$ is the \gls{enr} (in dB) of the noise source, ranging from \SIrange{14}{16}{\decibel} across the band, and $T_\mathrm{OFF}=\SI{290}{\kelvin}$ corresponds to the physical temperature of the reference load (ambient). This yields $T_\mathrm{ON}\approx \SI{9550}{\kelvin}$. The receiver noise temperature $T_\mathrm{r}[k]$ is then derived using the Y-factor method:
\begin{equation}
	T_\mathrm{r}[k] = \frac{T_\mathrm{ON} - Y[k]\,T_\mathrm{OFF}}{Y[k] - 1}, 
	\quad \text{with} \quad 
	Y[k] = 10^{\frac{P_\mathrm{hot}[k] - P_\mathrm{cold}[k]}{10}},
\end{equation}
where $P_\mathrm{hot}[k]$ and $P_\mathrm{cold}[k]$ are the measured powers in dBm. Note that $Y[k]$ represents the ratio of linear powers, calculated here from the difference of logarithmic measurements.

To mitigate small-scale spectral fluctuations caused by quantization noise and impedance mismatch ripple, the derived $T_\mathrm{r}[k]$ was smoothed by fitting a fifth-order polynomial in frequency $\nu_k$:
\begin{equation}
	T_\mathrm{r}^{\mathrm{fit}}[k] = \sum_{m=0}^{5} a_m \nu_k^m.
\end{equation}
The coefficients $a_m$ were obtained via least-squares regression over the valid passband (\SIrange{0.3}{2.4}{\giga\hertz}). Figure \ref{fig:rx_temperature_caren} shows the measured and fitted receiver temperatures.

With the receiver response characterized, the calibrated sky temperatures were derived by comparing the measured sky power $P_\mathrm{sky}[k]$ to the cold-load reference $P_\mathrm{cold}[k]$. Assuming the receiver operates in its linear regime, the sky temperature is given by:
\begin{equation}
	T_\mathrm{sky}[k] = 
	\left(T_\mathrm{amb} + T_\mathrm{r}^{\mathrm{fit}}[k]\right) 
	10^{\frac{P_\mathrm{sky}[k] - P_\mathrm{cold}[k]}{10}} - T_\mathrm{r}^{\mathrm{fit}}[k],
\end{equation}
where $T_\mathrm{amb}=\SI{290}{\kelvin}$ is the physical temperature of the reference load during the cold calibration. 

The resulting spectrum is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:rfi_caren}, alongside a reference measurement taken at Cerro~Calán (where the Universidad de Chile Astronomy Department is located). The sky temperature at Cerro~Calán is significantly elevated due to urban RFI, reaching levels around \SI{e5}{\kelvin}. In contrast, Laguna~Carén exhibits a much lower baseline, approximately \SIrange[range-phrase=--]{2e2}{3e2}{\kelvin}, indicating a substantially cleaner radio environment. However, strong RFI features are evident, particularly at \SI{416}{\mega\hertz}, \SI{435}{\mega\hertz}, and in the \SIrange{530}{700}{\mega\hertz} range (digital TV band). These signals reach brightness temperatures approaching \SI{e8}{\kelvin}, posing a saturation risk. The current front-end incorporates bandpass filters to attenuate out-of-band interference, with additional filtering planned for future iterations.

\section{LO tone generation}
To validate the LO generation module, we performed measurements using an external spectrum analyzer connected directly to the RFSoC DAC output and a high-speed oscilloscope. The objective was to assess the spectral purity and frequency accuracy of the digitally synthesized signals. The spectrum analyzer was configured to capture a discrete trace of $N=750$ points over a \SIrange{3.28}{2457.6}{\mega\hertz} span. The DAC output was set to its maximum level for 14-bit samples to maximize the \gls{snr}. Figure \ref{fig:lo_osc_time} shows the time-domain waveform of the DAC output captured by the oscilloscope.

    \label{fig:lo_osc_time}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:lo_loopback_spectrum} presents the measured power spectrum. The four main tones appear at the expected frequencies. Although the spectrum analyzer's resolution bandwidth was insufficient to resolve individual frequency bins perfectly, a DAC-ADC loopback test using the same sampling frequency and internal FFT configuration confirmed that the generated tones coincide exactly with the synthesized frequencies, free of spectral leakage. The amplitudes are well matched across channels, with a slight power roll-off observed at higher frequencies, likely attributable to cable attenuation. The strongest spurious components remain below \SI{-57}{\decibelm}; the five most significant spurs are observed at \SIlist{533.9;268.6;1451.1;799.2;1716.4;946.6}{\mega\hertz}. These appear to be intermodulation products of the main tones with themselves and/or harmonics of the clock synthesizers.

To quantify the spectral purity, we computed dynamic metrics from the measured discrete power spectrum $P[k]$ (in dBm), where $k$ denotes the frequency bin index. The median noise floor across the band is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\mathrm{floor}} = \operatorname{median}_{k}\{P[k]\}.
\end{equation}
Spectral peaks were identified by finding the set of indices $\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{peak}}$ where the power exceeded a threshold defined relative to the floor:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{peak}} = \left\{ k \mid P[k] > P_{\mathrm{floor}} + \SI{10}{\decibel} \right\}.
\end{equation}
From this set, the four indices corresponding to the fundamental LO tones form the carrier index set $\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{tone}} \subset \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{peak}}$, while the remaining indices constitute the spurious set $\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{spur}} = \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{peak}} \setminus \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{tone}}$. We define the worst-case reference levels for the desired signals and spurs as:
\begin{align}
    P_{\mathrm{carrier}} &= \min_{k \in \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{tone}}} P[k], \\
    P_{\mathrm{spur}} &= \max_{k \in \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{spur}}} P[k].
\end{align}
Finally, the \gls{sfdr} is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{SFDR} = P_{\mathrm{carrier}} - P_{\mathrm{spur}}.
\end{equation}
The resulting value, $\mathrm{SFDR} = \SI{39.31}{\decibel}$, demonstrates that the spectral purity of the synthesized tones is sufficient for their application as local oscillators in the \gls{fdm} system. Nevertheless, external amplification and filtering stages are required to achieve the target output power levels (see \S\ref{sec:analog_system}) and further suppress spurious content before the mixing stage.

\section{Correlation tests}
To rigorously assess the end-to-end performance of the digital backend, two sets of correlation tests were conducted under controlled laboratory conditions. In the first test, an amplified noise source was distributed to all chains via an 8-way splitter, providing a common broadband input to each signal chain. In the second test, eight prototype CHARTS antennas were simultaneously connected to the FDM system. These experiments were designed to verify phase coherence (within a certain amount of time) across all channels and to evaluate the correlation of the multiplexed signals.

After initial power level adjustment, we performed a complex data capture with a time resolution of \SI{10}{\milli\second}, acquiring a total of \num{20000} complex spectra (corresponding to a $\sim$\SI{3}{\minute} integration) to ensure adequate statistics for correlation analysis. Using the calibration routine described in \S\ref{sec:re-quantization}, we obtained the digital gain coefficients for all \num{8192} frequency channels. Finally, we computed the time-averaged \gls{cps} (visibility) for all antenna pairs:
\begin{equation}
    S_{ij}[k] = \frac{1}{N_\mathrm{acc}}\sum_{n=0}^{N_\mathrm{acc}-1} X_i[k, n] X_j^*[k, n],
\end{equation}
where $X_i[k, n]$ is the complex FFT output for chain $i$ at frequency channel $k$ and time index $n$, and $N_\mathrm{acc}$ is the total number of accumulated time samples. The phase was obtained as:
\begin{equation}
    \phi_{ij}[k] = \angle S_{ij}[k].
	\label{eq:cps_phase}
\end{equation} 
Figure~\ref{fig:noise_source_correlation_matrix} shows the resulting correlation matrix. The diagonal elements represent the autocorrelations (power, i.e. zero phase), while the off-diagonal elements show the \gls{cps} phases.

\section{Solar transit observation}
\label{sec:solar_transit}
On October 15, 2025, an on-sky solar observation was conducted using the CHARTS prototype antenna and analog electronics at the Laguna Carén site. The goal of this test was to validate the interferometric fringe rate expected for a short East–West baseline and to confirm phase coherence across the full digital signal chain for an astronomical source outside the laboratory. Before describing the observational setup, we summarize the theoretical framework that establishes the expected behavior of solar fringes in a two-element interferometer.

\subsection{Theoretical expectations}

Within the CHARTS science band of \SIrange{300}{500}{\mega\hertz}, the corresponding wavelengths span \SIrange{0.6}{1}{\meter}. For a baseline of length $b$, the angular fringe spacing is
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\rm fringe} \approx \frac{\lambda}{b}.
\end{equation}
For $b\simeq\SI{20}{\meter}$ this yields
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\rm fringe}\approx
\begin{cases}
\SI{2.9}{\degree}, & \nu=\SI{300}{\mega\hertz},\\
\SI{2.1}{\degree}, & \nu=\SI{400}{\mega\hertz},\\
\SI{1.7}{\degree}, & \nu=\SI{500}{\mega\hertz},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
all substantially larger than the solar diameter ($\theta_\odot\simeq\SI{0.5}{\degree}$). The Sun therefore acts as an unresolved source for this baseline, making it suitable for verifying fringe rates without spatial decoherence.

The interferometric phase of a two-element array is given by
\begin{equation}
\phi(t)=\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{s}(t)=2\pi\nu\,\tau_g(t),
\end{equation}
where $\tau_g(t)$ is the geometric delay. Temporal evolution arises from Earth's rotation, which imparts a time dependence to the source vector $\mathbf{s}(t)$. The fringe rate is the time derivative of the phase, $\dot\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,\mathbf{b}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{s}}(t)$. Since the source remains fixed on the celestial sphere while the Earth rotates with angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega_\oplus}$, the source velocity vector is given by the cross product $\dot{\mathbf{s}}(t) = \boldsymbol{\omega_\oplus}\times\mathbf{s}(t)$. The magnitude of this tangential velocity depends on the source's declination $\delta$ as $|\dot{\mathbf{s}}| = \omega_\oplus \sin(90^\circ - \delta) = \omega_\oplus\cos\delta$, representing the source's motion along a circle of constant declination. Consequently, for a baseline with a projection $b_{\rm EW}$ along the East–West direction (parallel to the instantaneous velocity vector $\dot{\mathbf{s}}$ at meridian crossing, see Figure \ref{fig:fringe_vectors}), the dot product simplifies to
\begin{equation}
\dot\phi \approx \frac{2\pi b_{\rm EW}}{\lambda}\,\omega_\oplus\cos\delta.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[t]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\textwidth]{fringe_vectors.png}
	\caption[Geometry of fringe-rate vectors]{Schematic geometry used to derive the fringe rate. The unit source vector $\mathbf{s}(t)$ and its apparent velocity $\dot{\mathbf{s}}(t)=\boldsymbol{\omega_\oplus}\times\mathbf{s}(t)$ (red) due to Earth's rotation are shown. The baseline vector $\mathbf{b}$ is indicated (put in celestial equator for simplification but for East-West arrangement this doesn't really matter because the projection $b_{\rm EW}$ is equal at all latitudes); $\delta$ is the source declination. The fringe rate is proportional to $\mathbf{b}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{s}}(t)$.}
	\label{fig:fringe_vectors}
\end{figure}

Using $b_{\rm EW}\simeq\SI{20}{\meter}$, $\omega_\oplus = 2\pi/\SI{23.9345}{\hour}$ (sidereal day) and the solar declination during the test ($\delta_\odot\simeq -8.5^{\circ}$), the predicted fringe rates are
\begin{equation}
\dot\phi \simeq
\begin{cases}
\SI{32.54}{\radian\per\hour}, & \nu=\SI{300}{\mega\hertz},\\
\SI{43.38}{\radian\per\hour}, & \nu=\SI{400}{\mega\hertz},\\
\SI{54.23}{\radian\per\hour}, & \nu=\SI{500}{\mega\hertz},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
corresponding to $\sim 5$, $7$, and $9$ fringes per hour. These theoretical values guide the expected temporal behavior during the \SI{\sim30}{\minute} engineering observation.

\subsection{Data processing and fringe-rate extraction}

For each frequency channel $k$, the instantaneous \gls{cps} ($S_{12}[k, n] = X_1[k, n] X_2^*[k, n]$) was computed and averaged over $\sim$\qty{10}{\second} (\num{1000} complex samples) intervals to increase the \gls{snr}. The visibility phase (Eq.~\ref{eq:cps_phase}) was then calculated as a function of time index $n$, yielding $\phi[k, n]$. The phase time series was unwrapped to remove $2\pi$ discontinuities, yielding a smooth function $\tilde\phi[k, n]$. A linear model was then fitted with respect to the physical time $t_n$:
\begin{equation}
   \tilde\phi[k, n] \approx \dot\phi[k]\,t_n + \phi_0[k],
\end{equation}
where $\dot\phi[k]$ is the observed fringe rate (in rad/s) for channel $k$. Its uncertainty $\sigma_{\dot\phi}$ was obtained from the least-squares covariance.

The projected East--West baseline was then estimated from:
\begin{equation}
   b_{\rm EW}[k] = 
   \frac{\dot\phi[k]\,\lambda_k}{2\pi\,\omega_\oplus\cos\delta},
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_k = c/\nu_k$ is the wavelength of channel $k$. The uncertainty is given by:
\begin{equation}
   \sigma_{b} = 
   \frac{\sigma_{\dot\phi}[k]\,\lambda_k}{2\pi\,\omega_\oplus\cos\delta},
\end{equation}
and the number of fringes per hour obtained via:
\begin{equation}
   \text{fringes/hour} = \frac{|\dot\phi[k]|}{2\pi}\times 3600.
\end{equation}

\section{100GbE transmission integrity}
\label{sec:100gbe_test}
To validate end-to-end 100GbE data transmission from the RFSoC to the processing node, we implemented an optical-fiber test using the RFSoC’s QSFP28 interface and a remote server equipped with an Intel E810-CAM2 100GbE network interface card. Both devices were configured with matching MAC addresses, IP addresses, and UDP ports.

During initial testing, the server failed to recognize the link on interface \texttt{enp112s0np0}. This issue was traced to the QSFP28 module being held in reset through the RFSoC GPIO signal \texttt{qsfp\_rst}, which prevents the laser from activating. A small GPIO-driven reset pulse was added to the firmware, allowing the module to initialize properly and the optical link to come up successfully.

With the physical link established, we verified connectivity by monitoring link-status registers and implemented a packet sequence counter in the RFSoC. A custom software receiver captured incoming packets and validated their sequence numbers to detect packet loss or corruption. Initially, no valid packets were received despite the link being established. After tuning the \texttt{idle\_cycles} parameter to values greater than 980 clock cycles, the transmission stabilized and valid packets with a length of 5482 bytes (42 bytes IP/UDP header + 64 bytes sequence number header + 5376 bytes payload for 8 antennas) began to appear. A continuous one-minute acquisition revealed a systematic packet loss of 5--6\%. The measured data rate (\SI{12.43}{\giga\bit\per\second}) and packet rate (\SI{283397}{pkt/s}) fall slightly below their expected values of \SI{13.15}{\giga\bit\per\second} and \SI{300000}{pkt/s}, respectively (Figures~\ref{fig:data_rate_plot} and \ref{fig:packet_rate_plot}).

Analysis of the sequence numbers revealed a highly regular loss pattern: approximately 40 consecutive valid packets followed by 2--3 lost packets. Such determinism points to a timing mismatch between the programmable logic domain (\SI{307.2}{\mega\hertz}) and the GTY transceiver domain (\SI{156.25}{\mega\hertz}). Their asynchronous relationship can cause periodic misalignment in the detection of packet boundaries inside the 100GbE core, resulting in dropped frames.

However, it is important to note that the packet capture was performed using Wireshark\footnote{\url{https://www.wireshark.org/}}, whose capture buffers and timestamping constraints may also contribute to apparent packet loss. Frameworks such as \texttt{kotekan} and \texttt{DPDK} offer greater flexibility, larger buffer sizes, and kernel-bypass capture mechanisms. As such, part of the observed loss may originate from limitations in the packet-capture software rather than from the FPGA transmission itself.

Nevertheless, to mitigate genuine transmission-side packet loss, we are evaluating the following strategies:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Introduce an asynchronous FIFO} between the 307.2~MHz logic domain and the 156.25~MHz GTY domain to fully decouple the two clocks. This would prevent aliasing effects at packet boundaries, though it requires a custom crossing domain clock design not presently provided in the CASPER/System Generator toolflow.
    
	\item \textbf{Reprogram the GTY reference clock} via the onboard Skyworks SI5395B (see Figure \ref{fig:rfsoc_clocking_scheme}) to a sub-multiple of \SI{307.2}{\mega\hertz}, such as \SI{153.6}{\mega\hertz}, thereby improving timing coherence between the two domains.
    
    \item \textbf{Adjust the F-engine sample rate} to operate at a frequency commensurate with the GTY reference clock (e.g., 5 GSPS), improving domain alignment. This approach, however, would require redesigning the FDM frequency layout.
    
\end{enumerate}
Despite these losses, the test successfully established a stable 100GbE optical link and demonstrated complete end-to-end transmission from the RFSoC to the processing node. Figure~\ref{fig:corr_fdm_fiber} presents the correlation matrix obtained from the same noise-source test configuration (with chain~2 using a longer cable in this instance) over the fiber link. The results confirm correct channel selection, proper chain reordering, and stable cross-correlation performance. Notably, the fiber-based system achieved superior correlation quality compared to the BRAM-based capture approach, despite using significantly shorter integration times (1 second versus 3 minutes), demonstrating both the efficiency and reliability of the 100GbE data path.

	\caption[Correlation matrix from noise source test over 100GbE fiber link]{Correlation matrix obtained from the noise source test over the 100GbE fiber link. It shows the time averaged cross-correlation phases ($\angle \ S_{ij}$) between all antenna pairs over a 1-second (300,000 spectra) integration over the full 300-500 MHz bandwidth.}
	\label{fig:corr_fdm_fiber}
\end{figure}


The same packetizer architecture is intended for the full 32-antenna system, although 100GbE tests at this scale have not yet been performed due to current hardware limitations and the lack of a fully operational X-engine (and because tools like Wireshark can reliably measure only up to $\sim$\SI{20}{\giga\bit\per\second} with its I/O graph). Nonetheless, the successful demonstration of stable transmission and accurate correlation in the 8-antenna prototype strongly indicates that the design will scale to the 32-antenna configuration, provided that timing-alignment adjustments are implemented. Full validation at the 32-antenna data rate will be carried out once the \texttt{DPDK}/\texttt{kotekan} ingestion framework is deployed and operational.
