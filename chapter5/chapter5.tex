\chapter{Testing, validation, and performance results}
\label{chap:results}
This chapter presents the testing, validation, and performance results of the F-engine developed for the CHARTS project. It covers RFI characterization at the Laguna Carén site, LO tone generation validation, correlation tests with noise sources and prototype antennas, a solar transit observation, and 100\,GbE transmission integrity verification. Most tests were conducted in an 8-antenna configuration due to hardware limitations at the time of writing. Nevertheless, the full 32-antenna firmware has been implemented and awaits integration with the X-engine. A portion of this work, particularly the solar transit observation, has been accepted for publication at IEEE EuCAP 2026 \citep{CHARTS_Antenna_FDM_2026}.


\section{Site RFI characterization}
\label{sec:rfi_measurements}
On August 28, 2025, a specialized RFI measurement campaign was conducted to evaluate the complete digitization bandwidth (\SI{0}{\mega\hertz}–\SI{2457.6}{\mega\hertz}), aiming to detect potential spectral leaks at higher frequencies and see the impact of local RFI sources in the 300--500~MHz science band. For this analysis, the CHORD feed (an ultra-wideband antenna with $S_{11} < -10$~dB in the \qtyrange{300}{1500}{\mega\hertz} range, see \citealt{MacKay_2023} for detailed feed specifications) was employed alongside the RFSoC~4x2 backend and an amplification box with a well-defined system temperature, ensuring accurate characterization across the entire frequency range. The measurement setup is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:rfi_measurement_setup}. The procedure involved pointing the CHORD feed towards zenith and recording the power spectrum over a 15-minute integration period, with 25~ms of time resolution (dynamic spectra are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dynamic_spectra_15_min}). The recorded data were then calibrated to derive the sky temperature as a function of frequency, accounting for the known system temperature of the amplification box.

The raw power spectra were processed using a radiometric calibration procedure based on the classical \emph{Y-factor} method \citep[see][Section 3.6.6]{essential_radio_astronomy}. Each dataset consisted of accumulated power spectra (sum of squared magnitudes) from the FFT outputs. The raw accumulated counts for frequency channel $k$, denoted as $D_k$, were converted to power units in dBm\,MHz$^{-1}$ via:
\begin{equation}
	P_k = 10 \log_{10}\!\left(\frac{D_k}{N_\mathrm{acc}}\right) - P_\mathrm{cal},
\end{equation}
where $N_\mathrm{acc}$ is the accumulation length (number of spectra averaged in the FPGA), set to 7500 corresponding to a \SI{25}{\milli\second} integration time, and $P_\mathrm{cal}$ is a calibration offset empirically determined by injecting a \SI{400}{\mega\hertz} tone of known power.

To characterize the receiver noise temperature, measurements were performed using a calibrated noise source. We define two states: \emph{hot} (noise source ON) and \emph{cold} (noise source OFF). The effective brightness temperature for the hot state is given by:
\begin{equation}
	T_\mathrm{ON} = T_\mathrm{OFF}\left(10^{\frac{\mathrm{ENR}}{10}} + 1\right),
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{ENR}$ is the excess-to-noise ratio (in dB) of the noise source, ranging from \SIrange{14}{16}{\decibel} across the band, and $T_\mathrm{OFF}=\SI{290}{\kelvin}$ corresponds to the physical temperature of the reference load (ambient). This yields $T_\mathrm{ON}\approx \SI{9550}{\kelvin}$. The receiver noise temperature $T_{\mathrm{rx},k}$ is then derived using the Y-factor method:
\begin{equation}
	T_{\mathrm{rx},k} = \frac{T_\mathrm{ON} - Y_k\,T_\mathrm{OFF}}{Y_k - 1}, 
	\quad \text{with} \quad 
	Y_k = 10^{\frac{P_{\mathrm{hot},k} - P_{\mathrm{cold},k}}{10}},
\end{equation}
where $P_{\mathrm{hot},k}$ and $P_{\mathrm{cold},k}$ are the measured powers in dBm. Note that $Y_k$ represents the ratio of linear powers, calculated here from the difference of logarithmic measurements.

To mitigate small-scale spectral fluctuations caused by quantization noise and impedance mismatch ripple, the derived $T_{\mathrm{rx},k}$ was smoothed by fitting a fifth-order polynomial in frequency $\nu_k$:
\begin{equation}
	T_{\mathrm{rx},k}^{\mathrm{fit}} = \sum_{m=0}^{5} a_m \nu_k^m.
\end{equation}
The coefficients $a_m$ were obtained via least-squares regression over the valid passband (\SIrange{0.3}{2.4}{\giga\hertz}). Figure \ref{fig:rx_temperature_caren} shows the measured and fitted receiver temperatures.

With the receiver response characterized, the calibrated sky temperatures were derived by comparing the measured sky power $P_{\mathrm{sky},k}$ to the cold-load reference $P_{\mathrm{cold},k}$. Assuming the receiver operates in its linear regime, the sky temperature is given by:
\begin{equation}
	T_{\mathrm{sky},k} = 
	\left(T_\mathrm{amb} + T_{\mathrm{rx},k}^{\mathrm{fit}}\right) 
	10^{\frac{P_{\mathrm{sky},k} - P_{\mathrm{cold},k}}{10}} - T_{\mathrm{rx},k}^{\mathrm{fit}},
\end{equation}
where $T_\mathrm{amb}=\SI{290}{\kelvin}$ is the physical temperature of the reference load during the cold calibration. 

The resulting spectrum is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:rfi_caren}, alongside a reference measurement taken at Cerro~Calán (where the Universidad de Chile Astronomy Department is located). The sky temperature at Cerro~Calán is significantly elevated due to urban RFI, reaching levels around \SI{e5}{\kelvin}. In contrast, Laguna~Carén exhibits a much lower baseline, approximately \SIrange[range-phrase=--]{200}{300}{\kelvin}, indicating a substantially cleaner radio environment. However, strong RFI features are evident, particularly at \SI{416}{\mega\hertz}, \SI{435}{\mega\hertz}, and in the \SIrange{530}{700}{\mega\hertz} range (digital TV band). These signals reach brightness temperatures approaching \SI{e8}{\kelvin}, posing a saturation risk. The current front-end incorporates bandpass filters to attenuate out-of-band interference, with additional filtering planned for future iterations.

\section{LO tone generation}
To validate the LO generation module, we performed measurements using an external spectrum analyzer connected directly to the RFSoC DAC output and a high-speed oscilloscope. The objective was to assess the spectral purity and frequency accuracy of the digitally synthesized signals. The spectrum analyzer was configured to capture a discrete trace of $N=750$ points over a \SIrange{3.28}{2457.6}{\mega\hertz} span. The DAC output was set to its maximum level for 14-bit samples to maximize the \gls{snr}. Figure \ref{fig:lo_osc_time} shows the time-domain waveform of the DAC output captured by the oscilloscope.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics{lo_osc_time.pdf}
    \caption[Time domain capture of the LO generation module output]{Time domain capture of the LO generation module output using the RIGOL MSO5104 oscilloscope. The waveform displays the superposition of the four synthesized tones at \SIlist{1065.6;1332;1600.8;1864.8}{\mega\hertz}.}
    \label{fig:lo_osc_time}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:lo_loopback_spectrum} presents the measured power spectrum. The four main tones appear at the expected frequencies. Although the spectrum analyzer's resolution bandwidth was insufficient to resolve individual frequency bins perfectly, a DAC-ADC loopback test using the same sampling frequency and internal FFT configuration confirmed that the generated tones coincide exactly with the synthesized frequencies, free of spectral leakage. The amplitudes are well matched across channels, with a slight power roll-off observed at higher frequencies, likely attributable to cable attenuation. The strongest spurious components remain below \SI{-57}{\decibelm}; the five most significant spurs are observed at \SIlist{533.9;268.6;1451.1;799.2;1716.4;946.6}{\mega\hertz}. These appear to be intermodulation products of the main tones with themselves and/or harmonics of the clock synthesizers.

To quantify the spectral purity, we computed dynamic metrics from the measured discrete power spectrum $P_k$ (in dBm). The median noise floor across the band is defined as:
\begin{equation}
	P_{\mathrm{floor}} = \operatorname{median}\{P_k\}.
\end{equation}
Spectral peaks were identified by finding the set of indices $\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{peak}}$ where the power exceeded a threshold defined relative to the floor:
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{peak}} = \left\{ k \mid P_k > P_{\mathrm{floor}} + \SI{10}{\decibel} \right\}.
\end{equation}
From this set, the four indices corresponding to the fundamental LO tones form the carrier index set $\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{tone}} \subset \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{peak}}$, while the remaining indices constitute the spurious set $\mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{spur}} = \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{peak}} \setminus \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{tone}}$. We define the worst-case reference levels for the desired signals and spurs as:
\begin{align}
	P_{\mathrm{carrier}} &= \min_{k \in \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{tone}}} P_k, \\
	P_{\mathrm{spur}} &= \max_{k \in \mathcal{K}_{\mathrm{spur}}} P_k.
\end{align}
Finally, the \gls{sfdr} is computed as:
\begin{equation}
	\mathrm{SFDR} = P_{\mathrm{carrier}} - P_{\mathrm{spur}}.
\end{equation}
The resulting value, $\mathrm{SFDR} = \SI{39.31}{\decibel}$, demonstrates that the spectral purity of the synthesized tones is sufficient for their application as local oscillators in the \gls{fdm} system. Nevertheless, external amplification and filtering stages are required to achieve the target output power levels (see \S\ref{sec:analog_system}) and further suppress spurious content before the mixing stage.

\section{Correlation tests}
To rigorously assess the end-to-end performance of the digital backend, two sets of correlation tests were conducted under controlled laboratory conditions. In the first test, an amplified noise source was distributed to all chains via an 8-way splitter, providing a common broadband input to each signal chain. In the second test, eight prototype CHARTS antennas were simultaneously connected to the FDM system. These experiments were designed to verify phase coherence (within a certain amount of time) across all channels and to evaluate the correlation of the multiplexed signals.

\subsection{Noise source test}
\label{sec:noise_source_test}

This setup ensured that all FDM chains received similar noise signals (only varying cable length), allowing for direct comparison of correlation performance. The analog setup of this test is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:noise_source_test_setup}, where we had the Agilent 346B noise source connected to the same amplification box (without the highpass filter) used in the RFI measurement, before being split and fed into the RFSoC~4x2 backend. Chain 1 had a longer cable between splitter and FDM, to introduce a known delay.

After initial power level adjustment, we performed a complex data capture with a time resolution of \SI{10}{\milli\second} (limited by software BRAM-based capture), acquiring a total of \num{20000} complex spectra, corresponding to a $\sim\SI{3}{\minute}$ integration, minimum time where the system showed clear stability. Using the calibration routine described in \S\ref{sec:re-quantization}, we obtained the digital gain coefficients for all \num{8192} frequency channels. Finally, we computed the time-averaged \gls{cps} for all chain pairs:
\begin{equation}
    (S_{ij})_k = \frac{1}{N_\mathrm{acc}}\sum_{n=0}^{N_\mathrm{acc}-1} (X_i)_{kn} (X_j)_{kn}^*,
\end{equation}
where $(X_i)_{kn}$ is the complex FFT output for chain $i$ at frequency channel $k$ and time frame $n$, and $N_\mathrm{acc}=\num{20000}$ is the total number of accumulated time samples. The phase was obtained as:
\begin{equation}
    (\phi_{ij})_k = \angle (S_{ij})_k.
	\label{eq:cps_phase}
\end{equation} 
Figure~\ref{fig:noise_source_correlation_matrix} shows the resulting correlation matrix. The diagonal elements represent the autocorrelations (power, i.e. zero phase), while the off-diagonal elements show the \gls{cps} phases.

Since all chains receive the same noise source, the expected phase difference between any pair should be approximately zero, except for chain~1, which has a longer cable introducing a known delay and a clear phase offset. Small, frequency-independent phase shifts are observed in other chains due to cable length differences and analog component variations, particularly in the FDM board. These offsets are common and correctable through calibration. The key result is that the system exhibits stable phase coherence across all chains, validating the digital processing integrity required for interferometric observations.

\subsection{Theoretical expectations}

Within the CHARTS science passband of \SIrange{300}{500}{\mega\hertz}, the corresponding wavelengths span \SIrange{0.6}{1}{\meter}. For a baseline of length $b$, the angular fringe spacing is
\begin{equation}
    \theta_{\rm fringe} \approx \frac{\lambda}{b}.
\end{equation}
For $b\simeq\SI{20}{\meter}$ this yields
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\rm fringe}\approx
\begin{cases}
\SI{2.9}{\degree}, & \nu=\SI{300}{\mega\hertz},\\
\SI{2.1}{\degree}, & \nu=\SI{400}{\mega\hertz},\\
\SI{1.7}{\degree}, & \nu=\SI{500}{\mega\hertz},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
all substantially larger than the solar diameter ($\theta_\odot\simeq\SI{0.5}{\degree}$). The Sun therefore acts as an unresolved source for this baseline, making it suitable for verifying fringe rates without spatial decoherence.

The interferometric phase of a two-element array is given by:
\begin{equation}
\phi(t)=\frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{s}(t)=2\pi\nu\,\tau_g(t),
\end{equation}
where $\tau_g(t)$ is the geometric delay. Temporal evolution arises from Earth's rotation, which imparts a time dependence to the source vector $\mathbf{s}(t)$. The fringe rate is the time derivative of the phase, $\dot\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}\,\mathbf{b}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{s}}(t)$. Since the source remains fixed on the celestial sphere while the Earth rotates with angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega_\oplus}$, the source velocity vector is given by the cross product $\dot{\mathbf{s}}(t) = \boldsymbol{\omega_\oplus}\times\mathbf{s}(t)$. The magnitude of this tangential velocity depends on the source's declination $\delta$ as $|\dot{\mathbf{s}}| = \omega_\oplus \sin(90^\circ - \delta) = \omega_\oplus\cos\delta$, representing the source's motion along a circle of constant declination. Consequently, for a baseline with a projection $b_{\rm EW}$ along the East–West direction (parallel to the instantaneous velocity vector $\dot{\mathbf{s}}$ at meridian crossing, see Figure \ref{fig:fringe_vectors}), the dot product simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
\dot\phi \approx \frac{2\pi b_{\rm EW}}{\lambda}\,\omega_\oplus\cos\delta.
\end{equation}

\begin{figure}[t]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{fringes_vectors.pdf}
	\caption[Geometry of fringe-rate vectors]{Geometric configuration illustrating the derivation of the interferometric fringe rate. The diagram shows the unit source vector $\mathbf{s}(t)$ pointing toward the celestial source, the baseline vector $\mathbf{b}$ connecting the two antennas, and the apparent velocity $\dot{\mathbf{s}}(t)=\boldsymbol{\omega_\oplus}\times\mathbf{s}(t)$ (red arrow) induced by Earth's rotation at angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega_\oplus}$. The source declination is denoted by $\delta$. The fringe rate is determined by the projection $\mathbf{b}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{s}}(t)$.}
	\label{fig:fringe_vectors}
\end{figure}

Using $b_{\rm EW}\simeq\SI{20}{\meter}$, $\omega_\oplus = 2\pi/\SI{23.9345}{\hour}$ (sidereal day) and the solar declination during the test ($\delta_\odot\simeq -8.5^{\circ}$), the predicted fringe rates are:
\begin{equation}
\dot\phi \simeq
\begin{cases}
\SI{32.54}{\radian\per\hour}, & \nu=\SI{300}{\mega\hertz},\\
\SI{43.38}{\radian\per\hour}, & \nu=\SI{400}{\mega\hertz},\\
\SI{54.23}{\radian\per\hour}, & \nu=\SI{500}{\mega\hertz},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
corresponding to $\sim 5$, $7$, and $9$ fringes per hour. These theoretical values guide the expected temporal behavior during the \SI{\sim30}{\minute} engineering observation.

\subsection{Data processing and fringe-rate extraction}

For each frequency channel $k$, the instantaneous \gls{cps} ($(X_1)_{kn} (X_2)_{kn}^*$) was computed and averaged over $\sim$\qty{10}{\second} (\num{1000} complex samples) intervals to increase the \gls{snr}. The visibility phase (Eq.\ \ref{eq:cps_phase}) was then calculated as a function of time index $n$, yielding $\phi_{kn}$. The phase time series was unwrapped to remove $2\pi$ discontinuities, yielding a smooth function $\tilde{\phi}_{kn}$. A linear model was then fitted with respect to the physical time $t_n$:
\begin{equation}
   \tilde{\phi}_{kn} \approx \dot{\phi}_k\,t_n + (\phi_0)_k,
\end{equation}
where $\dot\phi_k$ is the observed fringe rate (in \unit{\radian\per\second}) for channel $k$. Its uncertainty $\sigma_{\dot\phi_k}$ was obtained from the least-squares covariance.

The projected East--West baseline was then estimated from:
\begin{equation}
   b_{\mathrm{EW}, k} = 
   \frac{\dot\phi_k\,\lambda_k}{2\pi\,\omega_\oplus\cos\delta},
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_k = c/\nu_k$ is the wavelength of channel $k$. The uncertainty is given by:
\begin{equation}
   \sigma_{b} = 
   \frac{\sigma_{\dot\phi_k}\,\lambda_k}{2\pi\,\omega_\oplus\cos\delta},
\end{equation}
and the number of fringes per hour obtained via:
\begin{equation}
   \text{fringes/hour} = \frac{|\dot\phi_k|}{2\pi}\times 3600.
\end{equation}

\section{100\,GbE transmission integrity}
\label{sec:100gbe_test}
To validate end-to-end 100\,GbE data transmission from the RFSoC to the processing node, we implemented an optical-fiber test using the RFSoC's QSFP28 interface and a remote server equipped with an Intel E810-CAM2 100\,GbE \gls{nic}. Both devices were configured with matching MAC addresses, IP addresses (10.17.16.11 for the RFSoC and 10.17.16.10 for the server), and UDP destination ports (port 60000) to establish a direct point-to-point link.

During initial testing, the server failed to recognize the physical link on network interface \texttt{enp112s0np0} (the \gls{pcie} device name assigned to the 100\,GbE port in the E810-CAM2 NIC). Link detection requires active optical transmission from the QSFP28 module, which in turn depends on proper initialization of its internal laser driver. Investigation revealed that the QSFP28 transceiver was being held in a reset state through the RFSoC GPIO signal \texttt{qsfp\_rst} (active-low reset), which prevents the laser from activating and the module from establishing optical lock. To resolve this, a brief GPIO-driven reset pulse was added to the firmware initialization sequence: the \texttt{qsfp\_rst} signal is released high, allowing the QSFP28 module to complete its power-on self-test and enable the transmitter. Following this modification, the optical link was successfully established, with the server reporting ``link up'' status.

To quantify the achieved data rate, the received UDP packets were analyzed using \texttt{kotekan}. Each packet carries a sequence number $n$ in its header (see Table \ref{tab:ethernet_packet_structure}), incremented by the sender for each transmitted frame. By monitoring the sequence number progression over time, we verified that very few packets were dropped during sustained operation. Figure~\ref{fig:data_rate_sequence} shows the measured data rate over a representative \qty{240}{\second} observation window. The system maintained a stable throughput of $\sim\qty{52.63}{\giga bit\per\second}$, consistent with the theoretical rate required to transmit the payload and headers. Figure~\ref{fig:lost_packets} presents the packet loss statistics over the same interval, showing a mean loss rate of \qty{0.004}{\percent}. The occasional loss events are attributed to transient buffer overruns in the network stack rather than systematic transmission errors, and remain well within acceptable limits for interferometric data processing.
