
\chapter{Theoretical framework}
This chapter provides the theoretical foundation required to understand the work presented in this thesis. It is organized into several sections, each covering a key topic relevant to the design and implementation of the F-engine for CHARTS. The chapter begins with an introduction to radio astronomy fundamentals, followed by a description of time domain astronomy and transient astronomical phenomena such as \glspl{frb} and pulsars, including their main propagation effects. Next, it reviews essential concepts in digital signal processing, including sampling, quantization, filtering, polyphase filter banks, and frequency analysis using the discrete Fourier transform. The chapter then discusses spectrometers and correlators, outlining their main types and typical architectures. Finally, it presents an overview of the computational hardware used, with a focus on field programmable gate arrays and their integration with other processing units, such as graphical processing units, via high-speed optical interconnects. Also included is a description of the Xilinx AMD RFSoC 4x2 platform, which serves as the basis for the F-engine developed in this work.

\section{Radio astronomy fundamentals}
\label{sec:radio_fundamentals}
Radio astronomy, as its name suggests, is the branch of astronomy dedicated to observing celestial phenomena in the radio spectrum. The radio domain, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:radio_spectrum}, encompasses a substantial portion of the electromagnetic spectrum, spanning from kilometer wavelengths to mm/sub-mm wavelengths\footnote{Here microwaves are considered a subset of the radio spectrum.}. From the ground, however, observations are limited to the so-called radio window, which spans approximately from $\sim\qty{10}{\mega\hertz}$ to just below $\sim\qty{1}{\tera\hertz}$ in very dry places.
This broad range, spans nearly five orders of magnitude in frequency and encompasses a wide variety of physical emission mechanisms. Virtually all astronomical objects emit radio waves, which, unlike higher-frequency radiation, can penetrate dense gas and interstellar dust \citep[see][Chapter~1]{essential_radio_astronomy}. As a result, radio observations rarely suffer from absorption and have revealed a rich universe of phenomena, including radio galaxies, quasars, pulsars, radio transients, cold molecular clouds, the cosmic microwave background, cosmic magnetic fields, etc.

	\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{tikz figures/build/em_spectrum.pdf}
	\caption[Electromagnetic spectrum overview]{Overview of the electromagnetic spectrum, showing the relationship between wavelength ($\lambda$), frequency ($\nu$), and photon energy ($E=h\nu$). The spectrum is divided into regions such as radio, microwave (subset of radio), infrared (IR), ultraviolet (UV), X-ray, and $\gamma$-rays. The corresponding frequency and energy scales are indicated, along with common units for each region.}
	\label{fig:radio_spectrum}
\end{figure}

The accessibility of the radio spectrum from the ground is made possible by the Earth’s atmosphere, which is transparent across certain wavelength ranges but opaque in others. Figure \ref{fig:atmospheric_windows} illustrates the main atmospheric transmission windows. In the radio domain, the lower boundary of observability is set by the ionosphere, which reflects radiation below $\sim\qty{10}{\mega\hertz}$, while signals below $\sim\qty{2}{\mega\hertz}$ are absorbed by the ionized interstellar medium. The upper boundary is less sharply defined and extends into the mm/sub-mm regime, where water vapor and the troposphere strongly absorb radiation.

For wavelength regimes that are largely inaccessible from the ground, space-based observatories have proven essential. Missions such as Chandra \citep{Weisskopf2000} in the X-ray band, the James Webb Space Telescope \citep{JWST2006} in the optical and near infrared, and Planck \citep{theplanckcollaboration2006scientificprogrammeplanck} in the microwave regime have demonstrated the enormous scientific return of extending observations beyond atmospheric limitations, enabling transformative discoveries across astrophysics and cosmology \citep{Finkelstein_2023, Planck2020}. However, these achievements come at the cost of extremely complex engineering, limited instrument upgradability, and budgets that are orders of magnitude higher than those of ground-based facilities.

In this broader context, radio astronomy occupies a uniquely advantageous regime. Firstly, radio waves penetrate the atmosphere more effectively than most other bands, making ground-based radio astronomy particularly powerful. Also, at radio wavelengths, the photon occupation number is extraordinarily high compared to optical or higher-frequency bands \citep{Radhakrishnan1999}. This allows signals to be coherently detected and amplified while preserving phase information, in contrast to regimes where quantum noise fundamentally limits coherent measurements (see \citealt{RevModPhys.82.1155}). Furthermore, modern digital electronics provide highly stable frequency standards and nanosecond-level time tagging, well matched to the characteristic timescales of \si{\mega\hertz} radiation.

These properties enable the construction of large, distributed interferometric arrays with exceptional sensitivity and precision. Radio signals can be digitized with very high spectral resolution and frequency accuracy, supporting advanced techniques such as aperture synthesis and \gls{vlbi}. By effectively synthesizing apertures comparable to the size of the Earth, radio interferometers achieve angular resolutions far exceeding those attainable at other wavelengths, reaching the \si{\micro\arcsec} scale. A striking example is the $\sim\SI{20}{\micro\arcsec}$ image of the supermassive black hole in M87 produced by \citet{Akiyama_2019}.

\subsection{Relevant quantities in radio astronomy}
In radio astronomy, several fundamental quantities are required to describe and interpret the emission from astrophysical sources. These quantities connect the intrinsic radiation properties of the source with the signal detected by a radio telescope, which is affected by distance, frequency range and the telescope's properties such as aperture size and efficiency.

The first quantity to be defined is the specific intensity ($I_\nu$), which represents the received emission. It is defined as the power received per unit area, frequency, and solid angle:
\begin{equation}
	I_\nu \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{d}P}{ (\cos\theta \, \mathrm{d}\sigma) \, \mathrm{d}\Omega \, \mathrm{d}\nu}.
\end{equation}
where $\theta$ is the angle between the direction of the radiation and the observer's \gls{los}, $\mathrm{d}\sigma$ is the area element on the telescope surface, $\mathrm{d}\Omega$ is the solid angle element of the source, and $\mathrm{d}\nu$ is the frequency element. It is important to note that the specific intensity is an intrinsic property of the source, while the received power depends on the distance, effective aperture, observing bandwidth and integration time.


Building on the concept of specific intensity, it is essential to understand how thermal emission from astrophysical sources is described. At the beginning of the 20th century, Planck introduced the idea that electromagnetic radiation is emitted in discrete energy packets, or \emph{quanta}\footnote{Although Planck is historically credited with introducing the concept of energy quantization in \emph{quanta}, when he first formulated his radiation law, he regarded the discretization of energy as a mathematical trick rather than a fundamental physical principle. In fact, Planck himself did not initially believe that energy was inherently quantized; he considered it a pragmatic approach to fit experimental data, a ``desperate measure" in his own words (see \citealt{Planck1949-PLASAA-3} autobiography), to make his theory consistent with observations. Luckily, his conviction that the laws of thermodynamics should hold at all costs led him to stick to the idea of quantized energy levels.
}, laying the foundation for quantum theory and revolutionizing the understanding of blackbody radiation. The spectral brightness, which shares the same units as specific intensity, quantifies the power emitted per unit area, solid angle, and frequency by a blackbody at temperature $T$. This relationship is mathematically expressed by Planck's law \citep[see][Chapter~7]{StatisticalMechanics}:
\begin{equation}
	B_\nu(\nu, T) = \frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/kT}-1} ,
	\label{eq:planck}
\end{equation}
where $h$ is Planck's constant, $c$ is the speed of light, and $k$ is Boltzmann's constant. Planck's law has two notable limiting cases: at high frequencies ($h\nu \gg kT$), it reduces to Wien's law, while at low frequencies ($h\nu \ll kT$), it approaches the Rayleigh-Jeans approximation. 
Figure \ref{fig:spectral_brightness} illustrates the spectral brightness $B_\nu$ as a function of frequency\footnote{It is important to clarify that $B_\nu(\nu,T)$ and $B_\lambda(\lambda,T)$ are distinct quantities with different units, \si{\watt \per \steradian  \per \meter \squared \per \hertz} and \si{\watt \per \steradian \per \meter \cubed}, respectively. The conversion between them is given by $B_\nu = \frac{c}{\nu^2} B_\lambda$.} 
for a blackbody at 5500~K (approximately the temperature of the Sun's surface). It compares the predictions of Planck's law, the Rayleigh-Jeans approximation, and Wien's law, where the limiting behaviors are evident. Particularly the spectral brightness in the radio regime (below $\sim 10^{12}$ Hz) appears as a straight line in this log-log plot, indicating the validity of the Rayleigh-Jeans approximation in this frequency range.

The predictions of Planck's law, Rayleigh-Jeans approximation, and Wien's law are shown.}
	\label{fig:spectral_brightness}

If the radiation is in a \gls{lte}, the intensity behaves exactly as a blackbody (Eq.\ \ref{eq:planck}), $(I_\nu)_{\text{LTE}} \rightarrow B_\nu(T)$.
From this relationship and Planck's law given in Eq.\ \ref{eq:planck}, the brightness temperature can be obtained, which is defined as the temperature a blackbody should have to emit a specific intensity equal to that of the source at a given frequency:
\begin{equation}
	T_B = \frac{h\nu}{k \ln\left(1 + \frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2I_\nu}\right)}.
\end{equation}

In the radio regime, where $h\nu \ll kT$, the specific intensity can be approximated to (expanding the exponential as $e^{h\nu/kT_B} \approx 1 + \frac{h\nu}{kT_B}$):
\begin{equation}
	I_\nu = \frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2} \frac{1}{e^{h\nu/kT_B}-1} \approx \frac{2h\nu^3}{c^2} \frac{kT_B}{h\nu} = \frac{2kT_B\nu^2}{c^2}.
\end{equation}
Solving for the brightness temperature yields:
\begin{equation}
	T_B \approx \frac{I_\nu c^2}{2k\nu^2}.
\end{equation}
It is important to note that $T_B$ is not necessarily equal to the physical temperature $T$ of the source. These quantities can differ significantly, particularly in non-LTE conditions. Nonthermal processes exhibit frequency-dependent brightness temperatures without a well-defined physical temperature. For instance, synchrotron radiation from relativistic electrons in magnetic fields can produce brightness temperatures far exceeding the actual temperature of the emitting region, while thermal sources in LTE have brightness temperatures that closely match their physical temperature.

In any case, the main advantage of using brightness temperature in radio astronomy is that it provides a convenient way to express the intensity of radio sources in temperature units, which are often more intuitive and easier to relate to physical processes than power units.

While the specific intensity $I_\nu$ characterizes the intrinsic emission from an astronomical source, radio telescopes do not measure $I_\nu$ directly. Instead, they detect the specific flux density ($S_\nu$), which represents the total power received per unit area and frequency from the source, integrated over the solid angle subtended by the telescope's beam:
\begin{equation}
	S_\nu = \int I_\nu \, \mathrm{d}\Omega,
	\label{eq:flux_density}
\end{equation}
where the integration is performed over the solid angle subtended by the source. In radio astronomy, specific flux density is commonly measured in Janskys (Jy), where $1\,\mathrm{Jy} = 10^{-26} \ \si{\watt \per \meter \squared \per \hertz}$. This unit was named after Karl Jansky, who made the first detection of radio waves from an astronomical source in 1933 (see \citealt{Sullivan_1984} for more details on the story).

For point sources, where the angular size is much smaller than the telescope beam, the flux density provides a direct measure of the source strength. However, for extended sources that are resolved by the telescope, the relationship between flux density and brightness distribution becomes more complex, requiring careful consideration of the telescope's point spread function. Since our interest case in this thesis is the observation of compact sources (pulsars and \glspl{frb}), which can be treated as point sources for most radio telescopes, we will not delve deeper into this topic here. 

\subsection{Radiometers}
The concept of antenna temperature ($T_a$) provides a convenient way to characterize the strength of a received signal in radio astronomy. It is defined as the temperature that would generate the same noise power in a matched resistor as that collected by the antenna. Quantitatively, the antenna temperature can be expressed in terms of the received power and the bandwidth, following the Johnson–Nyquist relation (see \citealt{Nyquits_noise} for derivation):
\begin{equation}
    T_a = \frac{P}{k \, \Delta \nu},
	\label{eq:thermal_noise_power}
\end{equation}
where $P$ is the received power, $\Delta \nu$ is the bandwidth, and $k$ is Boltzmann’s constant. 

To measure this noise power, radio astronomers use a radiometer, a device specifically designed to detect and quantify extremely weak electromagnetic signals. The sensitivity of such instruments is characterized through the system temperature ($T_\text{sys}$) which incorporates all noise (including the source signal) contributions in the signal chain:  
\begin{equation}
    T_\text{sys} = T_a + T_{\text{rx}},
\end{equation}
where $T_{\text{rx}}$ is the effective noise temperature of the receiver and associated electronics.  

In practice, $T_{\text{rx}}$ can be decomposed into the contributions of multiple analog components. For a cascade of $m$ components, each with its own noise figure $F$ and gain $G$ (see Figure \ref{fig:friis}), the effective receiver noise is given by the Friis formula \citep{Friis1944}:  
\begin{equation}
    T_{\text{rx}} = T_1 + \frac{T_2}{G_1} + \frac{T_3}{G_1 G_2} + \dots + \frac{T_{m}}{G_1 G_2 \cdots G_{m-1}}.
	\label{eq:friis}
\end{equation}

Finally, the full system temperature can be expressed as the sum of all relevant contributions:  
\begin{equation}
    T_\text{sys} = T_{\text{rx}} + \underbrace{T_\text{src} + T_\text{cmb} + T_\text{sky} + T_\text{spill}+ \dots}_{T_a} \ ,
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $T_\text{src}$: contribution of the astronomical source of interest (generally very weak).
	\item $T_\text{cmb} \approx 2.73 \, \text{K}$: cosmic microwave background, constant in all directions.
	\item $T_\text{sky}$: atmospheric emission, strongly dependent on observing frequency and opacity.
	\item $T_\text{spill}$: spillover noise from radiation entering the feed in parabolical reflectors due to imperfect illumination or side lobes.
\end{itemize} 

To estimate the sensitivity of a radiometer, we use the radiometer equation \citep[see][Section~3.6.2]{essential_radio_astronomy}, which relates the statistical uncertainty of the noise measurement ($\sigma_T$) to the system parameters:  
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_T = k_c \, \frac{T_\text{sys}}{\sqrt{\Delta \nu \, t_\text{int}}},
	\label{eq:radiometer}
\end{equation}
where $t_\text{int}$ is the integration time, and $k_c$ is a proportionality constant that depends on the type of detector. Eq.\ \ref{eq:radiometer} is fundamental because it shows that sensitivity improves with larger bandwidth and longer integration.  

The \gls{snr} can then be written as:  
\begin{equation}
    \text{SNR} = \frac{T_a}{\sigma_T} = \frac{T_a}{T_\text{sys}} \sqrt{t_\text{int} \, \Delta \nu}.
\end{equation}
In radio astronomy, the astronomical signal is usually several orders of magnitude weaker than the noise. Therefore, long integration times, large bandwidths, and low-noise receivers are essential to improve detectability.

\subsection{Sensitivity and SEFD}
While the radiometer equation (Eq.\ \ref{eq:radiometer}) defines the sensitivity in terms of temperature fluctuations ($\sigma_T$), radio astronomers usually need to express this limit in terms of specific flux density ($S_\nu$), as defined in Eq.\ \ref{eq:flux_density}. To bridge the gap between the physical energy intercepted from the sky and the equivalent temperature detected by the electronics, we must introduce the effective collecting area of the telescope, $A_e$.

Assuming an unpolarized source (where the energy is equally split between two orthogonal polarizations) and a single-polarization feed, the power collected by the antenna is given by:
\begin{equation}
    P = \frac{1}{2} S_\nu A_e \Delta \nu.
    \label{eq:power_collected}
\end{equation}
By equating this collected power with the thermal noise power defined by the Johnson-Nyquist relation ($P = k T_a \Delta \nu$, from Eq.\ \ref{eq:thermal_noise_power}), we can derive the fundamental relationship between flux density and antenna temperature:
\begin{equation}
    k T_a \Delta \nu = \frac{1}{2} S_\nu A_e \Delta \nu \implies T_a = \left( \frac{A_e}{2k} \right) S_\nu.
    \label{eq:Ta_flux_relation}
\end{equation}
The term in parentheses is often referred to as the sensitivity, denoted here as $\Gamma$ (measured in $\text{K Jy}^{-1}$). It represents the efficiency of the telescope in converting incident flux density into antenna temperature:
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma = \frac{T_a}{S_\nu} = \frac{A_e}{2k}.
\end{equation}

Using this relation, we can define the \gls{sefd}, a critical figure of merit for radio telescopes. The \gls{sefd} is defined as the flux density of a source that would produce an antenna temperature equal to the system temperature ($T_\text{sys}$):
\begin{equation}
    \text{SEFD} = \frac{T_\text{sys}}{\Gamma} = \frac{2 k T_\text{sys}}{A_e}.
\end{equation}
A lower \gls{sefd} indicates a more sensitive system. By substituting $\sigma_T$ from the radiometer equation (Eq.\ \ref{eq:radiometer}) into the gain relationship, we can express the minimum detectable flux density ($\Delta S_{\text{min}}$) as:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta S_{\text{min}} = \frac{\sigma_T}{\Gamma} = \frac{k_c \, T_\text{sys}}{\Gamma \sqrt{\Delta \nu \, t_\text{int}}}.
\end{equation}
Expressing this in terms of \gls{sefd}:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta S_{\text{min}} = \frac{k_c \, \text{SEFD}}{\sqrt{\Delta \nu \, t_\text{int}}}.
    \label{eq:sensitivity_sefd}
\end{equation}

For aperture-type antennas (like parabolic reflectors), $A_e$ is typically related to the physical area $A_p$ through an aperture efficiency factor $\eta_{\text{ap}}$. However, for dipole-based arrays such as CHARTS, the effective area is more conveniently expressed in terms of the antenna gain $G(\theta, \phi)$ (see \citealt{stutzman2012antenna}, Section 2.5 for definition) relative to an isotropic radiator:
\begin{equation}
    A_e(\theta, \phi) = \frac{\lambda^2}{4\pi} G(\theta, \phi) \eta_{\text{rad}},
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the observing wavelength and $\eta_{\text{rad}}$ is the radiation efficiency. In these systems, $\eta_{\text{rad}}$ accounts for ohmic losses in the dipole elements and the ground plane, as well as impedance mismatch losses. 

In the context of an interferometric array with $N$ identical antennas, the total effective area increases by a factor of $N$ ($A_{e, \text{total}} = N A_e$), effectively reducing the \gls{sefd} by $N$ and improving the sensitivity of the combined array.

\subsection{Radio telescopes receivers}
The design of radio telescope receivers has undergone significant advancements since the 1940s, beginning with the introduction of the Dicke switch to mitigate gain fluctuations \citep{dicke1946}. By the 1960s, systematic studies, such as those by \citet{tiuri1964}, established a foundational framework for analog receiver architectures, emphasizing system noise temperature ($T_{\text{sys}}$) as a critical metric for sensitivity. 

Modern receivers predominantly employ heterodyne architectures, which integrate \glspl{lna}, frequency down-conversion via mixing with a \gls{lo}, and digitization. These designs, comprehensively described in \citet{Wilson2013_theory,Wilson2013_practical}, provide flexible frequency translation and stable gain control. Figure \ref{fig:heterodyne_receiver} illustrates a typical heterodyne receiver, encompassing the \gls{rf} front-end, mixer, LO, \gls{adc}, and decimation stages.

Despite the widespread use of heterodyne technology, there is a growing trend towards direct sampling receivers, enabled by the availability of high-speed, high-dynamic-range \glspl{adc}. In this approach, the sky signal (after amplification and appropriate filtering) is digitized directly at the \gls{rf} or at a high intermediate frequency without prior analogue down-conversion. This simplifies the analogue front-end, reduces sources of instrumental noise (less components in Eq.\ \ref{eq:friis}) and allows flexible digital signal processing.

\section{Time-domain astronomy and radio transients}
\label{sec:transient_astronomy}

Astronomy has traditionally focused on mapping the spatial and spectral properties of celestial sources, revealing the large-scale structure and composition of the Universe. Yet, a complementary perspective arises when one observes how these signals evolve in time. This approach, known as time-domain astronomy, explores the dynamic sky by studying variations in brightness, polarization, or frequency over timescales that range from fractions of a millisecond to years. Such temporal variability offers a unique window into energetic and transient astrophysical processes that cannot be accessed through static observations alone.

The study of time variability has historically led to major breakthroughs in astrophysics. The discovery of pulsars by \citeauthor{Hewish1968}\ in \citeyear{Hewish1968} revealed the existence of neutron stars, providing one of the first confirmations of stellar evolution beyond supernova explosions. Likewise, the detection of X-ray variability in Cygnus~X-1 offered early evidence for the presence of black holes \citep{CygnusX1_1972}. These discoveries demonstrated that time-series analysis is a powerful diagnostic of compact objects, extreme magnetic fields, and relativistic plasma environments.

Within this domain, a particularly rich class of phenomena is formed by transient sources that appear, brighten, and fade on observable timescales. Transients may last from milliseconds, as in \glspl{frb}, to months, as in supernovae and tidal disruption events. While some exhibit periodic or quasi-periodic behavior, others are one-off events whose mechanisms remain only partially understood. Their fleeting nature poses significant observational challenges, requiring continuous monitoring and rapid data processing to capture their signatures before they vanish.

For this thesis in particular we will focus on two types of radio emitting objects: \glspl{frb} and pulsars. Both classes emit bright, coherent radio pulses, but they may differ in their origins, timescales, and observational characteristics. First, we will review the main propagation effects that shape their observed signals.

\subsection{Propagation effects}
\label{sec:propagation_effects}
Radio waves traveling through ionized media interact with free electrons and magnetic fields, giving rise to several propagation effects (see \citealt{Lorimer2004}, Chapter 4):

\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Dispersion:} Caused by the frequency-dependent group velocity of radio waves in plasma, resulting in lower-frequency components arriving later than higher frequencies. The delay between two frequencies $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$ is proportional to the \gls{dm}, which has units of \si{\parsec \per \centi \meter \cubed} and is defined as the integrated column density of free electrons along the \gls{los}:
			\begin{equation}
				\mathrm{DM} = \int_0^L n_e(l) \, \mathrm{d}l \ ,
			\end{equation}
			where $n_e(l)$ is the electron density along the \gls{los} and $L$ is the path length. The explicit time delay is given by:
			\begin{equation}
				\Delta t = k_{\text{DM}} \, \mathrm{DM} \left( \nu_\text{ref}^{-2} - \nu^{-2} \right) ,
			\end{equation}
			with $k_{\text{DM}} = 1/(2.41\times10^{-4}) \ \si{\second \mega \hertz \squared \centi \meter \cubed \per \parsec }$ and $\nu_\text{ref} $ some reference frequency. Pulsars and FRBs both exhibit measurable dispersion sweeps, which are corrected through the process of dedispersion.
			
			When channelizing the signal into discrete frequency bins, dispersion causes intra-channel dispersion smearing, which is more acute at lower frequencies where dispersion is greater. This dispersion time $t_{\text{DM}}$ across a channel of width $\Delta \nu_\text{ch}$ centered at frequency $\nu$ is given by \citep{LiamConnor2019}:
			\begin{equation}
			t_{\text{DM}} = 8.3 \, \text{DM} \, \left( \frac{\Delta \nu_\text{ch}}{\SI{1}{\mega\hertz}} \right) \left( \frac{\nu}{\SI{1}{\giga\hertz}} \right)^{-3} \, \si{\micro\second}.
			\label{eq:intra_channel_smearing}
			\end{equation}

			\item \textbf{Faraday rotation:} As linearly polarized radio waves propagate through a magnetized plasma, their plane of polarization undergoes Faraday rotation. The polarization angle $\chi$ varies with observing frequency as:
			\begin{equation}
			\chi(\nu) = \chi_0 + \mathrm{RM}\frac{c^2}{\nu^2},
			\label{eq:faraday_rotation}
			\end{equation}
			where $\chi_0$ is the intrinsic polarization angle and \gls{rm} is the rotation measure, defined as:
			\begin{equation}
			\mathrm{RM} = \frac{e^3}{2\pi m_e^2 c^4} \int_0^L n_e(l) B_\parallel(l) \, \mathrm{d}l .
			\end{equation}
			Here, $e$ is the elementary charge, $m_e$ is the electron mass, $B_\parallel(l)$ and is the magnetic field component parallel to the \gls{los}.

			Faraday rotation arises because the left- and right-handed circular polarization components experience different refractive indices in a magnetized plasma, producing a phase delay that accumulates along the propagation path and leads to the characteristic $\nu^{-2}$ dependence of $\chi$ \citep{RM}. Measurements of RM therefore provide a sensitive probe of magnetized plasma in the intervening \gls{ism} and \gls{igm}.

			Interestingly, recent observations of FRB~20221022A revealed an S-shaped swing in the polarization position angle, closely resembling features commonly observed in pulsars. Although the nature of these sources will be discussed in later sections (\S\ref{sec:pulsars} and \S\ref{sec:frbs}), this similarity has been interpreted as suggestive evidence that at least some of them may share related emission environments or mechanisms \citep{2025Natur.637...43M}.

			\item \textbf{Scattering:} Density inhomogeneities in the ISM cause multipath propagation, producing an exponential scattering tail in the pulse profile. This effect is stronger at lower frequencies, with $\tau_{\text{sc}} \propto \nu^{-\alpha}$ and $\alpha \sim 4$ for Kolmogorov turbulence \citep{Rickett_1977}. The scattering timescale can be estimated empirically from the DM and observing frequency.

			\item \textbf{Scintillation:} Variations in electron density lead to constructive and destructive interference, causing intensity fluctuations over time and frequency. These effects provide insights into the small-scale structure of the ISM and, importantly, can be used as an astrophysical lens to constrain the emission-region size of radio transients \citep{Scintillation_FRB_Nimmo_2025}.
\end{itemize}
Figure \ref{fig:sim_galactic_magnetar} illustrates how these effects shape the observed signal. The panels show a simulated pulse from the Galactic magnetar SGR~1935+2154 \citep{Galactic_magnetar, Bochenek_2020}, a two-component pulse whose discovery was groundbreaking due to its resemblance to FRBs, strengthening the hypothesis that FRBs may originate from magnetars. The simulation, generated with \texttt{fitburst} \citep{fonseca2024modelingmorphologyfastradio}, assumes a DM of \SI{332.7}{\parsec \per \centi \meter^3} and a scattering timescale of \SI{0.759}{\milli \second} at \SI{600}{\mega \hertz}. Dispersion delays the pulse over several seconds across the band (left), while dedispersion recovers the intrinsic profile, still broadened by scattering (right).

\subsection{Pulsars}
\label{sec:pulsars}
Pulsars form a well-defined subclass of radio-emitting objects, distinct from the broader category of transients. They are rapidly rotating, highly magnetized neutron stars that emit beams of coherent radio waves from their magnetic poles. As the star spins, these beams sweep across the sky like a cosmic lighthouse, and if the Earth lies within their path, observers detect a sequence of periodic pulses. This periodicity, ranging from milliseconds to seconds, is one of the hallmarks that separates pulsars from one-off or irregular transients.

\begin{figure}[t]
	\centering
	\includegraphics{pulsar.pdf}
	\caption[Pulsar emission geometry]{Simplified pulsar emission geometry. A rapidly rotating neutron star with a misaligned magnetic axis emits beamed radio waves from its magnetic poles (shown as radio beams). As the pulsar spins around its rotation axis (spin axis), the beam sweeps across space like a lighthouse. When Earth lies within the beam's path, periodic radio pulses are detected. The magnetic field lines (shown in black) are anchored to the neutron star and co-rotate with it.}
	\label{fig:pulsar_geometry}
\end{figure}

Since their discovery in 1967, pulsars have become fundamental astrophysical laboratories. Their remarkable rotational stability makes them precise cosmic clocks, enabling tests of general relativity, the study of dense matter equations-of-state, and the characterization of the \gls{ism}. Moreover, pulsar timing arrays \citep{PulsarTimingArray} exploit their long-term stability to search for nanohertz gravitational waves \citep{Bernardo_2025}.

The emission mechanism of pulsars is thought to involve coherent processes in the magnetosphere, where charged particles accelerated along curved magnetic field lines produce radio waves \citep{mitra2024decodingnaturecoherentradio}. Despite decades of study, the detailed physics of pulsar emission remains an active area of research \citep{Melrose_2016}, particularly regarding the origin of phenomena such as mode changing, nulling, giant pulses, and glitches, sudden spin-up events attributed to internal dynamics of the neutron star crust and superfluid interior \citep{Antonopoulou2022,zubieta2024timingirregularitiesglitchespulsar}.

While their well-defined profiles and repeatability make some pulsars, such as the Crab pulsar \citep{B_hler_2014} embedded within the steady-source Tau~A, useful for calibration purposes, pulsars are generally not preferred as calibration sources due to their intrinsic variability. In radio astronomy, steady-source continuum emitters are typically favored for calibration.


\subsection{Fast radio bursts}
\label{sec:frbs}

\glspl{frb} are bright, millisecond-duration bursts of coherent radio emission of extragalactic origin, first reported in \citeyear{Lorimer_2007} by \citeauthor{Lorimer_2007} Their high \gls{dm} values, exceeding the expected Galactic contribution, demonstrate that they originate at cosmological distances.


The observational landscape has rapidly expanded: while the majority of FRBs are detected as one-off events, a subset exhibit repeating behavior, notably FRB~121102 \citep{Spitler_2016} which has been seen to have some periodicity \citep{Braga_2025}, suggesting multiple formation pathways. Proposed progenitor models range from compact objects such as magnetars and neutron star mergers to more exotic mechanisms (see \citealt{Zhang2023physics} and \citealt{EmissionMechanisms2022} for reviews on emission mechanisms). Figure~\ref{fig:frb_sky_distribution} presents the sky distribution of known FRBs in equatorial coordinates. While the distribution appears biased toward positive declinations due to the geographical concentration of existing surveys in the northern hemisphere, studies such as \citet{Josephy2021} demonstrate that FRBs are statistically isotropic when analyzed in Galactic coordinates, showing no dependence on Galactic latitude. According to the \href{https://blinkverse.zero2x.org/overview}{Blinkverse} database \citep{Blinkverse2023}, only 25\% of one-off FRBs and 7\% of repeating sources have been detected below \SI{0}{\degree} declination, confirming this observational bias rather than an intrinsic spatial preference.

\begin{figure}[t]
	\centering
	\includegraphics{frb_sky_distribution.pdf}
	\caption[Sky distribution of known FRBs]{Sky distribution of known FRBs in equatorial coordinates (RA/Dec), showing the locations of one-offs and repeating sources. Data from the \href{https://blinkverse.zero2x.org/overview}{Blinkverse} database \citep{Blinkverse2023}.}
	\label{fig:frb_sky_distribution}
\end{figure}

Detecting FRBs instrumentally demands backends with high time and frequency resolution, capable of performing real-time dedispersion. Large-scale surveys conducted by facilities such as \gls{chime} with its FRB backend \citep{CHIME/FRB_2018}, the Australian Square Kilometre Array Pathfinder (ASKAP; \citealt{ASKAPsystem2021}), and the Five-Hundred-Meter Aperture Spherical Telescope (FAST; \citealt{fast2011}) have collectively identified thousands of FRBs. These detections have facilitated population studies and host galaxy associations, as demonstrated in the first CHIME/FRB catalog \citep{CHIMEcatalog2021} and the commensal real-time ASKAP fast transient (CRAFT) incoherent-sum survey \citep{Shannon_2025}. Additionally, open-access datasets, such as FAST-FREX \citep{FAST-FREX}, provide dynamic spectra open to the community.

Cosmologically, FRBs are promising probes of the ionized baryon content of the Universe. The observed DM can be decomposed as:
\begin{equation}
	\mathrm{DM}_{\text{total}}(z) = \mathrm{DM}_{\text{MW}} + \mathrm{DM}_{\text{halo}} + \mathrm{DM}_{\text{IGM}}(z) + \sum_i \mathrm{DM}_{\text{galaxy}_i} + \mathrm{DM}_{\text{env}} .
	\label{eq:dm_total}
\end{equation}
Each term captures a distinct electron column along the line of sight, with contributions from the Milky Way disk and halo, the \gls{igm}, intervening galaxies, and the immediate environment of the source \citep{Macquart_2020}. The \gls{igm} component, in particular, encodes the distribution of baryons and the history of reionization and large-scale magnetization up to a redshift $z$. Consequently, localizing FRBs to their host galaxies and securing redshift measurements is essential for cosmological applications, as it enables separation of the individual contributions and calibration of the DM-redshift relation, Eq.\ \ref{eq:dm_total}. Despite rapid progress, only a few dozen FRBs have been precisely localized to date, including some one-off events via \gls{vlbi} \citep{Cassanelli_2024, 2025ApJS..280....6C}.


Nevertheless, fundamental questions remain open: the nature of progenitors, the emission mechanisms, and the influence of local environments. Next-generation facilities such as the Square Kilometre Array (SKA; \citealt{Weltman_2020}), the Deep Synoptic Array 2000 (DSA-2000; \citealt{hallinan2019dsa2000radiosurvey}) and the Canadian Hydrogen Observatory and Radio-Transient Detector (CHORD; \citealt{CHORD}) are expected to dramatically expand the FRB sample and deliver sub-arcsecond localizations, consolidating their role as astrophysical and cosmological probes.


\section{Digital signal processing fundamentals}
\Gls{dsp} involves the use of mathematical algorithms to analyze and manipulate signals in digital form. DSP is fundamental in spectrometer design, offering advantages over analog systems such as precise time delays, stable cross-correlation measurements, and predictable quantization effects. Digital systems provide high dynamic range, support multichannel outputs, and are completely independent to phase instability from environmental changes. They are also easier to replicate and maintain, especially in large arrays, except at extremely high data rates \citep[see][Chapter 8]{thompson2017interferometry}.


This section summarizes key DSP concepts relevant to this work: digital sampling, the Nyquist-Shannon theorem, quadrature sampling, quantization, frequency analysis using the \gls{dft}, and window functions, including polyphase filter banks. For a more comprehensive treatment of these topics, readers are referred to \citet{Proakis1996digital}, \citet{Price2016spectrometers} and \citet{Crockett2023}.

\subsection{Digital sampling}
Sampling is one of two processes that take place when an analog signal is converted into a digital equivalent (the other is quantization which will be covered in \S\ref{sec:quantization}). We can think of sampling as converting the time axis of a signal to a set of discrete time instants, while quantization converts its amplitude to a discrete set of representable amplitude values. This simple model of \gls{adc} conversion is depicted in Figure \ref{fig:adc_process}, where an analog signal is passed through an ADC to create a digital equivalent.

In the digital domain, as illustrated on the right side of Figure \ref{fig:adc_process}, the signal is represented at discrete time intervals, each separated by a sampling period $t_s$. The notation $x_n$ indicates discrete time indexing, where $n$ is an integer representing the sample number. The sampling rate $\nu_s$ and sampling period are inversely related:
\begin{equation}
	\nu_s = \frac{1}{t_s},
	\label{eq:sampling_frequency}
\end{equation}
where $\nu_s$ is expressed in Hz or samples per second (SPS). Therefore the sampling signal can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
	x_n \triangleq x(n t_s) .
\end{equation}

The selection of an appropriate sampling rate is determined by the frequency content of the signal under analysis. An insufficient sampling rate results in the loss of critical information and an inaccurate digital representation, mainly due to aliasing, an effect that will be discussed in more detail in \S \ref{sec:nyquist_shannon}. On the other hand, an excessively high rate increases computational demands without yielding significant benefits. Achieving an optimal balance is essential in digital signal processing applications, such as spectrometry, to ensure accurate spectral analysis without introducing aliasing or unnecessary noise. The following section addresses the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, which formalizes the criteria for proper sampling.

\subsection{Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem}
\label{sec:nyquist_shannon}
The Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem, originally proposed by \citet{Nyquist1928} and formalized by \citet{Shannon1949}, establishes the minimum sampling rate required to avoid aliasing. For a bandlimited signal with maximum frequency $\nu_{\text{max}}$, the sampling frequency must satisfy:
\begin{equation}
\nu_s > 2\nu_{\text{max}} .
\label{eq:nyquist_condition}
\end{equation}

The theorem applies equally to baseband signals (centered near 0~Hz) and bandpass signals (occupying a frequency range $[\nu_L, \nu_H]$ away from DC). For bandpass signals with bandwidth $\Delta\nu = \nu_H - \nu_L$, the condition becomes:
\begin{equation}
\nu_s > 2\Delta\nu ,
\end{equation}
enabling bandpass sampling, where aliasing is deliberately used to translate the signal into a lower frequency range.

The frequency spectrum is partitioned into Nyquist zones of width $\nu_s/2$ (Figure~\ref{fig:nyquist_zones}). A signal component in zone $m$ is mapped to the first zone according to:
\begin{equation}
\nu_{\text{alias}} =
\begin{cases}
\nu_{\text{tone}} \bmod \nu_\text{Nyquist} + \nu_0, & \text{even zones},\\
\nu_\text{Nyquist} - (\nu_{\text{tone}} \bmod \nu_\text{Nyquist}) + \nu_0, & \text{odd zones},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\nu_\text{Nyquist} = \nu_s/2$ and $\nu_0$ is the zone's lower boundary. When $\nu_s$ is insufficient, spectral components overlap in the first Nyquist zone, causing aliasing distortion.

\subsection{Quadrature sampling}
\label{sec:quadrature_sampling}
Quadrature sampling is a technique used to digitize a band-limited signal by shifting its spectrum so that it is centered at 0 Hz. Unlike conventional (real-valued) Nyquist sampling, quadrature sampling produces a complex-valued signal. In this representation, the sampling rate equals the signal bandwidth ($\nu_s = \Delta\nu$), meaning each complex sample contains the information of two real samples. This approach allows the representation of both positive and negative frequency components \citep{Price2016spectrometers}. 

In the context of quadrature sampling, negative frequencies arise naturally from the complex representation of signals. A real-valued signal has a symmetric spectrum (this is best explained after introducing the notion of \gls{ft}, see \S\ref{sec:dft}) meaning negative frequencies are redundant. However, when representing a signal as a complex baseband signal (such as Eq.\ \ref{eq:complex_signal}), the spectrum is no longer symmetric. Positive and negative frequencies now correspond to distinct components: positive frequencies represent signals rotating counterclockwise in the complex plane, while negative frequencies represent clockwise rotation. This distinction is crucial for quadrature sampling, as it allows the full bandwidth to be captured without aliasing, effectively doubling the information capacity compared to real sampling at the same rate.

To convert a real, Nyquist-sampled signal $x_n$ centered at 0 Hz into a quadrature-sampled signal, it can be multiplied by a complex exponential $e^{\mathrm{j}2\pi \nu_0 n}$, resulting in:
\begin{equation}
	\label{eq:quadrature_sampling}
	x^\prime_n = x_n e^{\mathrm{j}2\pi \nu_0 n} \, ,
\end{equation}
where $\nu_0$ is the center frequency of the signal. This multiplication shifts the spectrum of $x_n$ to be centered at $\nu_0$, allowing it to be sampled at a rate equal to its bandwidth. The resulting complex signal can be represented as:
\begin{equation}
	x^\prime_n = (x_I)_n + \mathrm{j} (x_Q)_n
	\label{eq:complex_signal}
\end{equation}
where $(x_I)_n$ and $(x_Q)_n$ represent the in-phase and quadrature components, respectively. In practice, this is implemented using a \gls{nco}, where a cosine wave is used for the in-phase component and a sine wave (90° phase-shifted) for the quadrature component, as illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:quadrature_sampling}.

\subsection{Quantization}
\label{sec:quantization}
Quantization is the process by which the continuous amplitudes of an analogue signal are mapped to a finite set of discrete levels (the second block in the ADC present in Figure \ref{fig:heterodyne_receiver}). For an $M$-bit converter, the number of quantization levels is $L = 2^M$, and the quantized signal can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
	\hat{x}_n = \text{round}\!\left(\frac{x_n}{\Delta}\right)\Delta \ ,
\end{equation}
where $\hat{x}_n$ is the quantized output, $\text{round}(\cdot)$ denotes rounding to the nearest integer and
$\Delta = 2A / 2^M$ is the step size for an input range $[-A, A]$. This rounding introduces quantization noise, which can be modeled as a uniform random variable in $[-\Delta/2, \Delta/2]$, with average power:
\begin{equation}
	P_q = \frac{\Delta^2}{12} = \frac{A^2}{3 \cdot 2^{2M}}.
\end{equation}

Increasing $M$ reduces $P_q$, but at the cost of higher data rates and storage requirements. Importantly, quantization is nonlinear: it distorts waveforms, alters frequency content, and biases statistical measures such as correlations. In radio interferometry, this leads to systematic errors and bias in both amplitude and phase, ultimately affecting source brightness and position estimates \citep{menaparra2018quantizationbiasdigitalcorrelators}.  

These issues are critical in radio astronomy, where weak celestial signals coexist with strong \gls{rfi}. To avoid clipping when the input exceeds the ADC range, high dynamic range is required. The theoretical dynamic range of an $M$-bit ADC is:
\begin{equation}
	\text{DR}_{\text{ideal}} = 6.02M + 1.76 \ \text{dB}.
\end{equation}

Real devices, however, are limited by nonidealities such as jitter and thermal noise. Their performance is better described by the \gls{enob}, obtained from the measured \gls{sinad}:
\begin{equation}
	\text{ENOB} = \frac{\text{SINAD} - 1.76}{6.02}.
\end{equation}

The \gls{sinad} is defined as the ratio of the total signal power (fundamental plus harmonics and noise) to the noise and distortion power alone \citep{ADCStandards}:
\begin{equation}
	\text{SINAD} = 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{P_{\text{signal}}}{P_{\text{noise+distortion}}} \right) \quad \text{dB}.
\end{equation}

Another key metric is the \gls{sfdr}, which compares the fundamental tone to the largest spurious component. It is computed as \citep{ADCStandards}:
\begin{equation}
	\text{SFDR} = 10 \log_{10} \left( \frac{P_{\text{fundamental}}}{P_{\text{spur, max}}} \right) \quad \text{dBc},
\end{equation}
where $P_{\text{spur, max}}$ is the power of the largest spurious tone excluding the fundamental. This is particularly relevant for mitigating intermodulation products caused by strong interferers.



\subsection{Fourier transform}
\label{sec:dft}
A large portion of signal processing is devoted to the problems of detection and estimation. Detection refers to deciding whether a particular signal set is present in the observation, while estimation concerns determining the parameters that describe it. In practice, observed signals are often corrupted by noise or interference, making these tasks non-trivial. A common strategy is to represent the signal in terms of a basis set that spans the signal space \citep{helstrom1968statistical}. For periodic signals, frequently encountered in engineering problems and radio astronomy, the natural basis consists of sines and cosines, leading directly to Fourier analysis.

Figure~\ref{fig:fourier_decomposition} illustrates this principle. A periodic time-domain signal (left) is expressed as a linear combination of sinusoidal functions at harmonically related frequencies. Each component contributes with a specific amplitude $b_n$, shown in the frequency domain (right) as discrete spectral lines located at multiples of the fundamental frequency $1/T$. This visualization emphasizes the duality between the time and frequency domains: while the time-domain signal may appear complex, its harmonic content can be concisely described by a small set of Fourier coefficients. This spectral representation is the foundation of many detection and estimation techniques in signal processing, as it isolates the periodic components embedded in noisy or corrupted observations.

The continuous \gls{ft} provides the foundation for frequency-domain analysis of signals. For a continuous-time signal $x(t)$, it is defined as \citep[see][Chapter 2]{Bracewell2000}:
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{F}\{x(t)\} = X(\nu) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-\mathrm{j}2\pi\nu t} \, \mathrm{d}t ,
\end{equation}
where $X(\nu)$ represents the frequency-domain representation of the signal. This transformation decomposes a time-domain signal into its constituent frequency components, providing a complete spectral description. 

The convolution of two functions $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
	\left[x(t) * y(t)\right](\tau) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) y(\tau - t) \, \mathrm{d}t ,
\end{equation}
where $*$ denotes the convolution operation. An important property of the \gls{ft} is that it maps convolution in the time domain to multiplication in the frequency domain, and inversely, multiplication in the time domain corresponds to convolution in the frequency domain. This relationship can be expressed mathematically as:
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{F}\{x(t) * y(t)\} = X(\nu) Y(\nu)  \quad \land \quad \mathcal{F}\{x(t) y(t)\} = X(\nu) * Y(\nu) ,
	\label{eq:convolution_theorem}
\end{equation}
where $X(\nu)$ and $Y(\nu)$ are the Fourier transforms of $x(t)$ and $y(t)$, respectively.

For discrete signals sampled at regular intervals, the \gls{dft} extends this concept to the digital domain. Working with $N$ uniformly spaced samples, the DFT produces $N$ equally spaced samples of the corresponding periodic spectrum, offering an elegant framework for spectral decomposition in an $N$-dimensional orthogonal vector space \citep{Cooley1969finite}. Its mathematical formulation is given by:
\begin{equation}
	X_k = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n e^{-\mathrm{j} 2 \pi k n / N}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1,
	\label{eq:dft}
\end{equation}
where $X_k$ are the DFT coefficients, $x_n$ are the input samples and $e^{-\mathrm{j} 2 \pi k n / N}$ are the basis functions. 
For real-valued input signals, the DFT output exhibits conjugate symmetry, meaning $X_{N-k} = X^*_k$, where the $^*$ denotes complex conjugation. This property allows only half of the spectrum to be retained, as the second half is simply the mirror image of the first. However, for complex input signals such as those obtained through quadrature sampling (see \S\ref{sec:quadrature_sampling}), this symmetry no longer holds, and all $N$ frequency bins must be computed to fully capture the spectral content. 

It is important to note that the DFT operates on finite sequences, whereas its ideal counterpart, the \gls{dtft}, is defined for infinite sequences. The \gls{dtft} is expressed as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{F}\{x_n\} = X(\nu) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x_n e^{-\mathrm{j} 2\pi\nu n}, \quad -1/2 \leq \nu < 1/2,
\label{eq:dtft}
\end{equation}
where $X(\nu)$ is a continuous function of the frequency variable $\nu$. The DFT can be interpreted as a sampled version of the \gls{dtft}, evaluated at discrete frequency points $\nu_k = k / N$.

While the DFT provides a rigorous framework for spectral analysis, its direct computation has $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ complexity, making it computationally expensive for large datasets. The fast Fourier transform algorithm (detailed in \S\ref{sec:fft}) reduces this to $\mathcal{O}(N \log_2 N)$, enabling real-time processing in radio astronomy applications. 

\subsection{The fast Fourier transform}
\label{sec:fft}
The DFT can be computed efficiently by a family of algorithms collectively known as the \gls{fft}, which reduce the arithmetic cost from $\mathcal{O}(N^2)$ to $\mathcal{O}(N\log_2 N)$ \citep{CooleyTukey1965,OppenheimSchafer2010}.

A canonical radix-2 decimation-in-time (DIT) step splits the $N$-point DFT into two $N/2$-point DFTs of the even and odd samples, which are then combined through butterfly operations:
\begin{align}
X_k &= X_{e,k} + w_N^k\, X_{o,k},\\
X_{k+N/2} &= X_{e,k} - w_N^k\, X_{o,k}, \qquad k=0,\dots,\tfrac{N}{2}-1,
\end{align}
where $w_N \triangleq e^{-\mathrm{j}2\pi/N}$, and $X_{e,k}$ and $X_{o,k}$ denote the $N/2$-point DFTs of even and odd inputs, respectively. Figure \ref{fig:fft_algorithm} illustrates this process. The decimation-in-frequency (DIF) form applies a dual factorization on the outputs; both lead to $\log_2 N$ stages of length-2 butterflies and twiddle multiplications.

For streaming hardware, pipelined radix architectures organize these stages so that one sample can be accepted each clock once the pipeline is filled. Two widely used forms are the single-path delay-feedback (R2SDF) and the multi-path delay-commutator (R2MDC) pipelines. These FFT engines are the workhorses of real-time spectrometers and correlators in radio astronomy.


\subsection{Window functions and spectral leakage}
\label{sec:window_functions}
The \gls{fir} filtering stage serves to attenuate frequency components outside the desired band. Without this filtering, out-of-band signals can introduce unwanted noise within the passband due to spectral leakage and scalloping loss effects associated with the DFT applied in subsequent processing steps \citep{HUANG202061}.

The fundamental origin of this leakage is rooted in the Fourier properties of finite-duration signals. As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:rectangular_fourier} for the continuous case, a rectangular pulse $x(t)$ in the time domain transforms into a sinc function $X(\nu)$ in the frequency domain, characterized by slowly decaying sidelobes.

Equivalent behavior is observed in the discrete domain by considering the definition of the DFT for a finite-length signal given in Eq.\ \ref{eq:dft}. By this definition, the DFT analyzes a signal only over a limited interval $n=0$ to $N-1$. Mathematically, this is equivalent to multiplying the infinite-length signal $x_n$ by a rectangular (or tophat) window function $\Pi_{N,n}$:
\begin{equation}
    \Pi_{N,n} =
    \begin{cases}
        1 & 0 \leq n \leq N-1 \\
        0 & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}.
\end{equation}
This implicit truncation has profound consequences in the frequency domain. By applying the convolution theorem (see Eq.\ \ref{eq:convolution_theorem}) to the definition of the DFT (Eq.\ \ref{eq:dft}), we observe that the measured spectrum $X_k$ is actually a sampled version of the convolution between the signal's ideal spectrum (the \gls{dtft}, Eq.\ \ref{eq:dtft}) and the window's response:
\begin{align}
    X_k &= \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n e^{-\mathrm{j} 2\pi n k / N} \\
        &= \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x_n \Pi_{N,n} e^{-\mathrm{j} 2\pi n \nu} \bigg|_{\nu = k/N} \\
        &= \left. \mathcal{F} \left\{ x_n \cdot \Pi_{N,n} \right\} \right|_{\nu = k/N} \\
        &= \left. \left( X(\nu) * W(\nu) \right) \right|_{\nu = k/N}  
\end{align}
where $X(\nu)$ and $W(\nu)$ are the \gls{dtft} of the underliying infinite input signal and the rectangular window, respectively. Since the \gls{ft} of a rectangular pulse is a sinc function (or strictly, a Dirichlet kernel in the discrete domain), the ideal spectral components are convolved with this function. The Dirichlet kernel is characterized by slowly decaying peaks known as sidelobes. High-amplitude sidelobes allow energy from strong spectral components to ``leak'' into adjacent frequency bins, obscuring weaker signals and distorting the spectrum. This phenomenon is known as spectral leakage.

To mitigate leakage, alternative windowing functions are applied. Unlike the rectangular window, which has abrupt discontinuities at the boundaries, these functions taper the signal edges smoothly to zero. This smoothing significantly reduces the amplitude of the sidelobes in $W(\nu)$, thereby improving the dynamic range of the spectrometer. However, this improvement comes at a cost: suppressing sidelobes broadens the main lobe of the window's response, which reduces the effective spectral resolution.

In practice, windowing is implemented by multiplying the signal $x_n$ by a weighting sequence $w_n$ prior to the transform:
\begin{equation}
    X_{w,k} = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} w_n x_n e^{-\mathrm{j} 2\pi n k / N}.
\end{equation}
Following the convolution theorem for the DFT \citep[see][Chapter~8]{OppenheimSchafer2010}, this time-domain multiplication corresponds to a circular convolution in the frequency domain:
\begin{equation}    
    X_{w,k} = \frac{1}{N} (W \circledast X)_k,
\end{equation}
where $W_k$ is the DFT of the window function, and $\circledast$ denotes circular convolution:
\begin{equation}
    (W \circledast X)_k = \sum_{m=0}^{N-1} W_m X_{(k - m) \bmod N}.
\end{equation}
The modulo operation $(k - m) \bmod N$ reflects the periodic nature of the DFT, ensuring that the convolution wraps around the sequence boundaries.

Several window functions have been designed to optimize the trade-off between sidelobe suppression and spectral resolution. Among the most widely used in digital spectrometry are the Von Hann (Hanning) and Hamming windows. Table \ref{tab:window_functions} lists common window functions along with their mathematical expressions.

\subsection{Polyphase filter banks}
\label{sec:pfbs}
As shown in \S\ref{sec:window_functions}, applying a window function before the DFT is a common method to reduce spectral leakage. However, this approach alone is insufficient in real-time digital spectrometers, where both efficiency and spectral purity are critical. The \gls{pfb} technique provides an elegant solution by combining windowing and decimation with the DFT in a computationally efficient way \citep{Harris1978}.

In a PFB, instead of feeding the DFT directly with $N$ samples, a longer block of length $Q = N \times P$ is considered, where $P$ is the number of taps. This block is multiplied by a window function $w_n$, often derived from a sinc function further tapered by a smoother function (e.g., Hanning or Hamming, see Table \ref{tab:window_functions}) to improve sidelobe suppression. The weighted block is then decomposed into $N$ polyphase sub-filters of length $P$, which are summed to form a filtered sequence. Finally, this sequence is processed by an $N$-point DFT, yielding spectral estimates with strongly reduced leakage.

The weighting/windowing can be thought of as a filtering process in which the elements of the window function are the filter coefficients. Mathematically, this process is given by
\begin{equation}
    h_{n + pN}, \quad p = 0,1,\ldots,P-1,
\end{equation}
where each sub-filter coefficient, $h_{n + pN}$, corresponds to a phase-shifted, decimated version of the prototype filter. Collectively, the $N$ sub-filters followed by the DFT stage constitute the PFB. This structure is sometimes referred to \textit{windowed pre-sum FFT}, highlighting its equivalence to overlapping windowed FFT methods but with improved implementation efficiency. Figure \ref{fig:pfb_diagram} illustrates the architecture of a PFB with $N$ channels and $P=3$ taps.

\section{Spectrometers and correlators}

In radio astronomy, the measurement of spectral power distributions is fundamental to characterizing both the intrinsic properties of celestial sources and the effects of the intervening medium. This task is performed by spectrometers and correlators, which transform time-domain signals into frequency-domain representations that reveal the physical processes governing radio emission \citep{Price2016spectrometers}.

A spectrometer is an instrument designed to measure the spectral composition of signals received from astronomical sources. Its primary output is the \gls{psd}, typically expressed in units of \si{\watt \per \hertz}, which quantifies the power per unit frequency interval. For a radio telescope with effective collecting area $A_e$, the measured PSD is proportional to the specific flux density $S_\nu$ (see Eq.\ \ref{eq:flux_density}) integrated over the antenna aperture:
\begin{equation}
	\text{PSD} \propto \int_{A_e} S_\nu \, \mathrm{d}\sigma,
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{d}\sigma$ is an infinitesimal area element. This spectral measurement provides critical insight into emission mechanisms, source temperatures, propagation effects, and the physical conditions of the emitting plasma.

A correlator, on the other hand, is a specialized instrument that extends this concept to interferometric arrays by computing the cross-correlation between signals from different antennas. Further details on correlation are provided in \S\ref{sec:cross_correlation}.

The theoretical underpinnings of both spectrometers and correlators are explored in \S\ref{sec:wiener-khinchin} and \S\ref{sec:cross_correlation}, where the Wiener-Khinchin theorem and the cross-correlation formalism are discussed.

\subsection{Wiener-Khinchin theorem}
\label{sec:wiener-khinchin}
There are two primary methods for calculating the PSD, also referred to as the power spectrum, of a signal. The power spectrum, denoted as $S_{xx}$, and the autocorrelation function, $R_{xx}$, of a waveform are connected through the Wiener-Khinchin theorem. This theorem establishes that for a stationary (constant mean and variance) and ergodic (statistically consistent over time) signal $x(t)$, the PSD and autocorrelation are related by
\begin{equation}
	S_{xx}(\nu) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} R_{xx}(\tau) e^{- \mathrm{j} 2\pi \nu \tau} \, \mathrm{d}\tau,
	\label{eq:continous-wiener-khinchin}
\end{equation}
where $\nu$ represents frequency and $\tau$ denotes the time lag. The autocorrelation function of an ergodic process is defined as \citep{Gubner_2006}
\begin{equation}
	R_{xx}(\tau) =  \langle x(t) x^*(t - \tau)\rangle = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{2T} \int_{0}^{T} x(t) x^*(t - \tau) \, \mathrm{d}t
	\label{eq:continous_autocorrelation}
\end{equation}
where $\langle \cdot \rangle$ denotes the expectation value, which is the average over time for a stationary signal. For discrete signals, the theorem can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
	S_{xx}(\nu) = \sum_{m=-\infty}^{\infty} \left\langle x_n x^*_{n - m} \right\rangle e^{-\mathrm{j}2\pi m \nu}
	\label{eq:discrete-wiener-khinchin}
\end{equation}
where $m$ is the time lag index and $\nu$ is the normalized frequency in the interval $[-1/2,1/2]$. Spectrometers that calculate the PSD using Eq.\ \ref{eq:discrete-wiener-khinchin} are known as autocorrelation spectrometers (from now on XF ACS). These spectrometers compute the autocorrelation function of the input signal first and then apply a DTFT to obtain the PSD. Using this definition in Eq.\ \ref{eq:discrete-wiener-khinchin} and the convolution theorem, the PSD can also be expressed as
\begin{equation}
	S_{xx}(\nu) = \left\langle \norm{X(\nu)}^2 \right\rangle
	\label{eq:discrete_psd}
\end{equation}
where $X(\nu)$ is the DTFT output at frequency $\nu$. This result shows that the PSD can be obtained directly by averaging the squared magnitude of the DFT output. This is significant because it enables the computation of the PSD from the DFT\footnote{It is important to clarify that the integration time must be sufficiently long to ensure that DFT and DTFT are approximately equal.} of the signal, eliminating the need to explicitly calculate the autocorrelation function which can be computationally expensive, especially for large arrays. This type of spectrometer is known as \gls{ft} filter banks (hereafter FX FTF spectrometers). Figure \ref{fig:psd_methods} illustrates the two methods for calculating the PSD of a signal, highlighting the differences between ACS and FTF spectrometers.

\subsection{Cross-correlation}
\label{sec:cross_correlation}
In radio interferometry, the fundamental observable is formed by combining signals from pairs of antennas observing the same astronomical source. Consider the two-element interferometer depicted in Figure \ref{fig:correlator_schematic}. A wavefront from a distant source, traveling in direction $\widehat{\mathbf{n}}$, arrives at antenna $X$ and antenna $Y$ at different times due to the spatial separation defined by the baseline vector $\mathbf{b}$.

This spatial separation introduces a geometric time delay, $\tau_g$, given by $\tau_g = \mathbf{b} \cdot \widehat{\mathbf{n}} / c$. Consequently, the signals recorded, $x(t)$ and $y(t)$, are essentially time-shifted versions of the same wavefront (plus noise). To measure this coherence and retrieve the source information, the system computes the cross-correlation function between the pair.

The cross-correlation operation is often denoted by the pentagram symbol ($\star$) and is mathematically defined as:
\begin{equation}
    R_{xy}(\tau)=\left[x(t) \star y(t)\right](\tau) = \lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{2T} \int_{-T}^{T} x(t) y^*(t - \tau) \, \mathrm{d}t \ ,
	\label{eq:cross_correlation_definition}
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the integration time, and $y^*(t - \tau)$ is the complex conjugate of the signal $y(t)$ delayed by $\tau$. Physically, this function peaks when the lag parameter $\tau$ equals the geometric delay $\tau_g$. The integral can also be expressed as a convolution, which is useful for system analysis:
\begin{align}
    \left[x(t) \star y(t)\right](\tau) 
    &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)\, y^*(t - \tau) \, \mathrm{d}t \\
    &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t)\, y^*(-(\tau - t)) \, \mathrm{d}t \\
    &= \left[x(t) * y^*(-t)\right](\tau)
    \label{eq:cross_correlation}
\end{align}
where $y^*(-t)$ denotes the time-reversed and conjugated version of $y(t)$. Applying the \gls{ft} to both signals, and using the convolution theorem, we obtain:
\begin{equation}
    x(t) \star y(t) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{F}} X(\nu) Y^*(\nu)
    \label{eq:cross_correlation_fourier}
\end{equation}
where $X(\nu)$ and $Y(\nu)$ are the \glspl{ft} of $x(t)$ and $y(t)$, respectively. The right-hand side of Eq.\ \ref{eq:cross_correlation_fourier}, defined as $S_{xy}(\nu) := X(\nu) Y^*(\nu)$, is known as the \gls{cps} or visibility. This result demonstrates that the \gls{cps} is simply the \gls{ft} of the cross-correlation function. In the special case of a single antenna where $X = Y$, this reduces to the Wiener-Khinchin theorem discussed previously. This relationship forms the theoretical foundation for cross-correlation spectrometry, allowing interferometers to measure spatial coherence in the frequency domain.

\subsection{Correlator architectures}
\label{sec:spectrometer_architectures}
Correlators are generally divided into two main categories. In a lag (or XF) correlator, the cross-correlation operation is performed first, followed by a \gls{ft} (analogous to an XF ACS system). Examples of radio telescopes using this architecture is the WIDAR correlator in the Very Large Array (VLA; \citealt{Perley_2009}) and the \gls{alma} correlator \citep{ALMA_correlator}.
Conversely, in an FX correlator, the \gls{ft} is applied before the cross-correlation. In this type of correlator, the digitized data stream from each antenna is first transformed into a frequency spectrum using a real-time FFT. For every antenna pair, the resulting complex frequency components are then multiplied to obtain the \gls{cps}. The computational load is dominated by the \gls{ft} stage, and the total number of operations scales with the number of antennas. In contrast, for a lag correlator, the computational effort is mainly determined by the number of antenna pairs. Therefore, the FX architecture is more hardware-efficient, particularly as the number of antennas increases (see Section 8.8.5 of \citealt{thompson2017interferometry}). Examples of contemporary radio telescopes employing FX correlators for FRB detection include CHIME/FRB \citep{CHIME/FRB_2018}, ASKAP \citep{Shannon_2025}, MeerKAT \citep{MeerKAT}, DSA-110 \citep{DSA110_Ravi_2023}, Galactic Radio eXplorer (GReX; \citealt{GReX}), Bustling Universe Radio Survey Telescope in Taiwan (BURSTT; \citealt{BURSTT}), etc.

\subsection{F-engine}
In an FX correlator, the F-stage (commonly referred to as the F-engine) is responsible for three main tasks: channelization, requantization with gain adjustment and corner turn with packetization. Channelization allows for frequency-specific signal corrections and facilitates efficient distribution of data throughout the correlator hardware.

As illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:f-engine}, the input signal is first digitized by the ADC, providing a discrete representation of the analogue voltages from each antenna. The PFB and FFT perform the channelization, dividing the wideband signal into narrow frequency bins with reduced spectral leakage. 

To reduce bandwidth and storage requirements, the spectra are re-quantized to a lower bit depth while maintaining adequate sensitivity. The corner turn operation then reorganizes the data: instead of being grouped by antenna, the output is arranged by frequency channel, which is the natural format required for the cross-correlation step. Finally, the packetizer encapsulates the data into network packets for efficient transmission to the X-engine, where the correlation products are computed.

\subsection{X-engine}
After the F-engine has channelized and packetized the data, the X-engine receives these packets, typically over high-speed network links. In modern radio telescopes, the X-engine is responsible for performing the complex cross-multiplication and accumulation of visibilities across all antenna pairs, forming the basis for imaging and calibration.
For an array with $N_\text{a}$ antennas, the number of total multiplications is $N_\nu N_\text{a}(N_\text{a}+1)/2$ (including autocorrelations), leading to a computational cost per spectral frame that scales as:
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{O} \left(N_\nu N_\text{a}^2\right),
\end{equation}
where $N_\nu$ is the number of frequency channels. Temporal integration only changes the multiplicative constant (the number of accumulations), not the asymptotic scaling (or computational complexity).

In addition to correlation, X-engines can perform beamforming by coherently summing antenna voltages with appropriate delays or phase rotations. A straightforward delay-and-sum implementation for $N_\text{b}$ beams has a computational complexity of:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{O}\left(N_\nu N_\text{b} N_\text{a}\right),
\end{equation}
which becomes prohibitive for large arrays. However, for regularly sampled arrays, FFT beamforming can be applied to synthesize approximately $N_\text{a}$ orthogonal beams with a reduced cost of:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{O}\left(N_\nu N_\text{a}\log_2 N_\text{a}\right),
\end{equation}
substantially improving efficiency when $ N_\text{b} \sim N_\text{a}$ \citep{Masui_2019}.
This approach, however, constrains the array geometry to a regular grid configuration, limiting its flexibility compared to direct beamforming, but is widely used in large-$N_\text{a}$, wide-field interferometers where real-time performance is critical \citep{Price_2024}.

\section{Computational hardware}
\label{sec:computational_hardware}
The digital signal processing required by modern radio telescopes relies primarily on four platforms: \glspl{cpu}, \glspl{gpu}, \glspl{fpga}, and \glspl{asic}. Each offers different trade-offs in terms of flexibility, efficiency, and development cost.  

\begin{itemize}
	\item \Glspl{asic} are custom-designed circuits optimized for a specific task. They powered major facilities such as the VLA and ALMA correlators \citep{Perley_2009,ALMA_correlator}. Although their fabrication is costly and inflexible, they provide unmatched power efficiency, making them attractive for large-scale projects \citep{D_Addario_2016}.  

	\item \Glspl{gpu}, originally developed for graphics rendering, have become widely used in high-performance computing. Their highly parallel architecture makes them ideal for large matrix operations and real-time data processing, often serving as backends for cross-correlation and imaging pipelines. In recent years their price has decreased significantly, especially due to the gaming and artificial intelligence industries, making them an attractive option for radio astronomy applications. Recent GPU architectures include tensor cores, specialized units originally designed for artificial intelligence workloads, which can accelerate correlation operations by a factor of five to ten compared to traditional GPU cores \citep{TensorCoreCorrelator}. This technology enables significant reductions in both computational cost and energy consumption, making GPUs increasingly competitive for large-scale signal processing. 
	An example of a pipeline specifically dedicated to \glspl{gpu} is the X-engine of CHIME \citep{Denman_2020}. 

	\item \Glspl{fpga} occupy a middle ground between flexibility and efficiency. They interface directly with \glspl{adc} and high-speed links, handling tasks such as channelization and packetization before passing data downstream. Their role in radio astronomy has been facilitated by open-source initiatives like the Collaboration for Astronomy Signal Processing and Electronic Research (CASPER; \citealt{hickish2016decadedevelopingradioastronomyinstrumentation}), and they remain the dominant choice for first-stage DSP. A dedicated discussion is provided in \S~\ref{sec:fpgas}. 
	\item \glspl{cpu} retain a central role as general-purpose processors. While less efficient for massively parallel tasks, they provide the control logic, coordination, and post-processing capabilities required in heterogeneous architectures that combine multiple platforms. 
\end{itemize} 

 
In practice, modern correlators employ hybrid solutions: \glspl{fpga} or \glspl{asic} for front-end channelization and packetization, followed by \glspl{gpu} or \glspl{cpu} clusters for cross-correlation and higher-level data products. This balance leverages the strengths of each technology to meet the demanding performance and scalability requirements of radio astronomy.

\subsection{Field programmable gate arrays}
\label{sec:fpgas}
\Glspl{fpga} are reconfigurable integrated circuits that offer a flexible hardware platform capable of being programmed in the field, with performance approaching that of \glspl{asic}, yet without the fabrication lead time and high cost of custom silicon \citep{kuon2008fpga}. They are programmed using \glspl{hdl} such as VHDL or Verilog, which allow designers to specify the logic and interconnections at a low level. For those new to FPGA development and HDL design, \citet{nandland2019fpga} provides a practical introduction to the fundamentals.

At their core, \Glspl{fpga} consist of a structured array of fundamental resources:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{\Glspl{clb}}, which include \glspl{lut} and registers for implementing arbitrary combinational and sequential logic.
  \item \textbf{Programmable interconnect fabric} that routes signals flexibly between CLBs and other blocks.
  \item \textbf{Dedicated specialized blocks}, such as DSP units for arithmetic operations and \gls{bram} for efficient data storage.
  \item \textbf{I/O blocks} that interface with external signals and support a variety of standards and voltage levels.
\end{itemize}

A typical \Gls{fpga} layout can thus be visualized as a matrix of CLBs  surrounded by I/O pads and interconnected via hierarchical routing networks. Each CLB (see Figure \ref{fig:clb_structure}) usually contains multiple LUTs and flip-flops, as well as multiplexers to support flexible logic structures, including arithmetic carry chains and synchronous timing.

The inclusion of specialized DSP blocks streamlines high-speed operations such as multiplication and accumulation, while \gls{bram} provides storage far more resource-efficient than distributed logic registers. Moreover, modern FPGAs often integrate additional features such as high-speed transceivers, \gls{pll}, and even embedded microprocessor cores or \gls{rfsoc} capabilities, enhancing their versatility for complex applications (see \citealt{zhang2018fpga}, Chapter 8).

FPGA architecture grants a compelling trade-off: designers can implement custom circuits with parallelism tailored to the application, while still retaining reconfigurability. Compared to fixed architectures like CPUs or GPUs, FPGAs achieve higher performance per \si{\watt} for many tasks, especially where spatial parallelism and low-latency processing are essential \citep{kuon2008fpga,zhang2018fpga}.

\subsection{Ethernet interconnects}

Ethernet has become the standard interconnect technology in modern radio astronomy backends, providing a scalable and cost-effective solution for transporting high data rates between FPGAs, GPUs, and storage systems \citep{Price_2020}. Commercial high-speed variants such as 10\,GbE (SFP+), 40\,GbE (QSFP+), and 100\,GbE (QSFP28; \citealt{IEEE8023bm2015}) are routinely employed, enabling real-time redistribution of digitized signals in FX correlators and spectrometers.  

A key design choice in these systems is the adoption of the \gls{udp} as the transport layer. UDP is lightweight and connectionless, avoiding the latency penalties of the transmission control protocol (TCP). Since astronomical signal processing prioritizes sustained throughput over guaranteed delivery, low levels of packet loss are acceptable. Typical packet headers include sequence numbers, antenna identifiers, and frequency channel indices, allowing receiving nodes to reassemble the data stream even when packets arrive out of order or are occasionally dropped.  

The corner turn operation reorganizes data from a time-major to a frequency-major arrangement: packets transmitted by F-engines contain time samples across all frequency channels for a single antenna, but cross-correlation nodes require all antennas' data for each frequency channel. UDP packetization and Ethernet switching enable this redistribution efficiently across the network, ensuring that each X-engine node receives the appropriate frequency channels from all antennas without requiring custom interconnects. This combination of Ethernet and UDP has thus become a defining feature of modern large-scale radio interferometers.


\subsection{RFSoC 4x2}
\label{sec:rfsoc}

The RFSoC 4x2\footnote{\url{https://www.realdigital.org/hardware/rfsoc-4x2}}, developed by RealDigital in collaboration with AMD-Xilinx, is a heterogeneous processing platform based on the Zynq UltraScale+ RFSoC Gen3 architecture. This board integrates high-speed \glspl{adc} and \glspl{dac} directly with FPGA fabric and embedded processors, making it particularly well-suited for real-time digital signal processing and software-defined radio (SDR) applications. A key advantage of the RFSoC 4x2 is its competitive pricing: it utilizes the same XCZU48DR SoC as the more expensive ZCU208 evaluation board but is offered at a significantly lower cost (\$\num{2499}), making it accessible for academic and research environments. This affordability, combined with its performance and flexibility, has positioned it as an attractive platform for educational purposes, enabling students and researchers to learn, prototype, and implement scientific instruments such as CHARTS. Internal UltraRAM and \gls{bram} further enhance buffering and streaming capabilities.

The board also provides multiple high-speed interfaces for data offload and system integration, including a 100\,GbE QSFP28 port, USB~3.0, and a Mini DisplayPort. External clock and \gls{pps} SMA ports enable precise synchronization and multi-board operation.
